# Melody Theory Foundations (the craft beneath "earworms")

The music theory of single-line melodic construction — what makes a line *sing* instead of
*wander* — from a verified research run (deep-research harness: 24 sources, 110 claims
extracted, 25 adversarially verified, 22 confirmed / 3 killed). This is the corrective to
the reductive "earworm = catchy hook + one surprise" framing. `melody_primacy.md` is the
pop/earworm angle; THIS is the common-practice and modal *theory*.

**Provenance tags:** `[V]` = verified in this run (cited, with vote). `[C]` = standard
common-practice canon (Fux 1725; Aldwell & Schachter; Caplin *Classical Form*; Kostka &
Payne; Schoenberg *Fundamentals*) — uncontroversial textbook material, not independently
re-verified here, flagged honestly. `[S]` = our own synthesis/application.

## 0. THE META-FINDING — a melody is a deep line elaborated

`[V, 3-0]` Beneath the surface, a strong melody is a **simple stepwise descent to the tonic,
prolonged**. Schenker's *Ursatz* pairs the **Urlinie** (fundamental line: a stepwise upper-
voice descent from a tonic-triad tone — `^3-^2-^1`, `^5-^4-^3-^2-^1`, or rarely `^8-…-^1`)
with the bass arpeggiation `I–V–I`; "neither can stand alone." Everything else is decoration
of that skeleton (passing/neighbor/arpeggiating tones = *prolongation*).

**The single most useful working test:** before elaborating, can you sing your theme's
skeletal descent to `^1`? If there is no goal-directed stepwise line to the tonic under the
surface, the melody **wanders by definition** — that is what "doodling on a piano" is, stated
in theory. (Source: Wikipedia *Fundamental structure*, quoting Schenker *Free Composition*.)

## 1. SINGLE-LINE CONSTRUCTION (Fux / species) — and the wandering diagnostic

`[V, 3-0]` The canonical rules of a well-formed line (cantus firmus), which double as the
diagnostic checklist for "does this resolve or wander":

- **Predominantly stepwise**, leaps occasional and mostly small. WHY: steps are maximally
  singable and open no tonal "gap."
- **Every note-to-note interval a melodic consonance.** Forbidden *as a melodic leap*:
  augmented intervals, diminished intervals, the tritone, **any 7th** — UNLESS a modal idiom
  owns that interval (§2). WHY: dissonant/augmented leaps are hard to intonate and *pull
  toward resolution* rather than continuation.
- **Gap-fill / leap-recovery** `[V, nuanced — the rigid form was REFUTED 0-3]`: a leap (esp.
  ≥ 4th) *tends to be* answered by a step in the opposite direction; ≤ 2 leaps in a row;
  consecutive same-direction leaps only if they **outline a triad**. Recovery may also be a
  return to the originating note or a smaller opposite leap (P5 then P4). Treat as a **strong
  heuristic, not law** — OMT itself calls the opposite-step "by far the most predominant
  solution," not the only one. WHY: a leap opens a perceptual gap listeners expect filled
  (Meyer gap-fill; Narmour Implication-Realization).
- **Balance the two contour dimensions:** rise vs. fall, and conjunct (step) vs. disjunct
  (leap). A long stepwise stretch is offset by a leap in the opposite direction; a leap is
  offset by steps.
- **Begin and end on `^1`; approach the final `^1` by step** — usually `^2-^1`, sometimes
  `^7-^1`, with the leading tone resolving to tonic (in minor-type modes the raised `^7`
  appears only at the cadence). **This is the literal diagnostic for closure vs. wandering:**
  a line that does not return decisively to `^1` by step reads as open/aimless.

**Recipe:** mostly 2nds; leap by 3rd/P4/P5/m6/octave occasionally; never aug-2/tritone/7th as
a leap (outside a modal idiom); recover leaps by step; end `…^2-^1` or `…^7-^1`.

> Sources: openmusictheory.github.io/cantusFirmus; viva.pressbooks.pub OMT species &
> first-species; smbutterfield IBMT. Descends from Fux *Gradus ad Parnassum* (1725).

## 2. INTERVAL TREATMENT & THE MODAL EXCEPTIONS

`[V]` **Consonance taxonomy:** perfect (P1/P5/P8), imperfect (M/m 3rds & 6ths) = consonant
melodic intervals; all 2nds, 7ths, and aug/dim intervals = dissonant. Pleasant/idiomatic
leaps are the consonant ones; the rest are stepwise-only or idiom-only.

`[V, 3-0]` **The modal exception:** the **double harmonic
scale** (`H – aug2 – H – W – H – aug2 – H` → `1 ♭2 3 4 5 ♭6 7`) has **two augmented seconds**
(`♭2→3` and `♭6→7`) and IS the Byzantine / Arabic Hijaz-Kar / Bhairav / Gypsy-major idiom.
The aug-2nd here is the **structural backbone and signature color, not an error.** Likewise the
harmonic-minor aug-2nd (`^6→^7`, e.g. F→G# in A minor). So:
- **Modal idiom (e.g. double-harmonic):** *feature* the aug-2nds as the line's
  fingerprint — they are consonant-by-idiom. The **tritone** (the "Devil's interval") becomes
  an available menace leap, used deliberately where common practice forbids it.
- **Tonal context:** obey the no-aug-2/tritone/7th-leap rule; expressive dissonance
  comes from *non-chord tones* (§5), not from leaping dissonant intervals.

**Preparing big/dissonant leaps:** approach from the opposite direction, place the leap's
landing on a chord tone / strong beat, and recover by step (§1).

> Sources: Wikipedia *Double harmonic scale*, *Augmented second*; Wikibooks *Consonance and
> Dissonance*.

## 3. HARMONIC IMPLICATION & THE CIRCLE OF FIFTHS  `[C — gap area; standard canon]`

(The run flagged this area as under-verified; the following is uncontroversial textbook
theory — Aldwell & Schachter, Kostka & Payne — to be applied, with that caveat.)

- **A single line implies harmony through its STRUCTURAL tones:** the notes on strong beats /
  long values are heard as chord tones; the rest decorate. Put the pitches you want the ear to
  harmonize **on the strong beats**.
- **Functional syntax:** Tonic → Pre-dominant (ii/IV) → Dominant (V/vii°) → Tonic. **Root
  motion by descending 5th is the strongest progression** (V→I, ii→V, vi→ii…); the **diatonic
  circle-of-fifths sequence** (I–IV–vii°–iii–vi–ii–V–I) is the canonical "engine" of forward
  harmonic motion.
- **Melodic sequence vs. harmonic sequence:** repeat a melodic pattern at a new pitch level
  (melodic sequence); when the *chords* also transpose by a consistent interval (usually down a
  5th / up a 4th), it's a harmonic sequence — the most directional way to *travel*.
- **Tonicization / secondary dominants:** approach any diatonic chord with its own `V` (or
  `vii°`) to momentarily make it a local tonic — the cheapest way to add harmonic richness
  under a stable tune.
- **"Let the harmony travel under a stable line"** `[S]`: hold or
  sequence a confident upper line while the *bass/chords* move by **chromatic mediant** or
  around the **circle of fifths** — the same melodic shape recolored by each new chord beneath
  it is the sensation of "a hero walking across a changing map."
- **Reharmonization** recolors the identical melody (jazz practice: tritone subs, modal
  interchange, chromatic mediants) — design the tune so it **survives re-harmonization**: a
  melody built mostly of common tones that fit many chords can be re-set under successive
  progressions without changing a note.

**Two melodist's orders (both valid; the failure was doing NEITHER — deferring harmony to "pending"):**
- *Progression-first:* sketch T–PD–D–T (descending-5th roots) FIRST, place chord tones on strong
  beats, decorate with NCTs.
- *Melody-first (the common working order):* (1) write the line freely, as if unharmonized; (2) lay a
  candidate progression under it; (3) NUDGE the structural tones (strong-beat / long notes) onto chord
  tones of each chord, leaving the pitches you displaced as passing/neighbor NCTs; (4) smooth the
  contour for voice-leading so the line connects by step instead of leaping to chase the chords.
  Iterate the chords and the nudges together. Either order, the OUTPUT is the same: a line whose
  strong-beat skeleton spells a nameable progression.

**The downbeat carries the root (by default).** The strong beat usually lands the chord ROOT — the most
stable, most legible read. A strong beat on the 3rd / 5th / 7th is an *intentional* color or mislead (a
5th-in-the-melody lilt, a 7th hung for tension, a deceptive read), used knowingly — not left to accident.
This is NOT a hard rule; it is the default the deviations play against. It is also what lets a repeated
melodic cell gain weight and variation: the SAME shape re-lands over a NEW chord each pass, so repetition
is recolored from beneath rather than reading as flat restatement (the I–V–vi–IV pop/VGM engine
is exactly this: a plain tune made to travel by the harmony under it).

**Operational — commit the implied progression; never leave it "pending."** A theme is not finished
until its strong-beat skeleton spells a specific progression you can write down. Leaving harmonization
"provisional/pending" is the concrete melody-writing failure that produces flat lines — the line is
built with no harmonic skeleton to give its strong beats meaning. Every theme DECLARES the progression
it exposes; the structural tones are checked against it (§8 item 5).

> Partial sources fetched: musictheory.pugetsound.edu *CircleOfFifths*; smbutterfield IBMT
> *diatonic circle-of-fifths*; beyondmusictheory *chromatic mediants*; learnjazzstandards
> *reharmonization*. Canonical: Aldwell & Schachter; Kostka & Payne.

## 4. PHRASE STRUCTURE & CADENCE RESOLUTION

`[V, 3-0]` **The cadence hierarchy is perceptually real** (Sears, Caplin & McAdams 2014;
trained musicians): completion **PAC > IAC > HC > Deceptive > Evaded.** Cadential strength
tracks how completely *both* harmonic and melodic closure are achieved.

- **PAC** (V–I root position, melody ends `^1`): closes both → **save for the END** of an
  8- or 16-bar unit.
- **IAC** (V–I root position but melody ends `^3` or `^5`): **harmonically closed, melodically
  OPEN** → `[V]` the natural **intermediate/non-final** cadence. *This is the theory behind
  "don't let the phrase resolve cleanly at bar 4."* Use an IAC or HC at the mid-point; the line
  is grounded but still wants to continue.
- **HC** (ends on V): harmonically open — a comma, the antecedent's question. `[C]`
- **Deceptive** (V–vi): denies the expected tonic — a surprise that *prolongs*. `[C]`
- **Plagal** (IV–I): *confirms* after a PAC, doesn't initiate closure. `[C]`
- **Phrygian half-cadence** (iv6–V in minor): the modal/Hijaz cadence. `[C]`

`[C — Caplin/Schoenberg]` **The two phrase archetypes:**
- **SENTENCE (8 bars):** *presentation* (a 2-bar basic idea + its repetition/variation) →
  *continuation* (**fragmentation** — shorter units — + rhythmic and **harmonic acceleration**)
  → *cadence*. The engine is acceleration toward the cadence.
- **PERIOD (8 bars):** *antecedent* (basic idea + contrasting idea, ends WEAK — HC or IAC) →
  *consequent* (parallel opening, ends STRONGER — PAC). Question / answer.

`[C]` **Avoiding squareness:** vary phrase lengths (not all 4+4); use **phrase expansion**
(extend the cadence), **elision** (the last bar of one phrase is the first of the next, so the
seam never fully stops), and cross-barline ties/anticipations so phrases *breathe* across the
hypermetric grid instead of parking on every downbeat. **This is "phrasing":** deploying
cadence strength and length with judgment so the music has commas, semicolons, and periods —
not a full stop every four bars.

> Sources: mcgill.ca MPCL *Sears/Caplin/McAdams 2014*; tnp.mtsnys.org *Mackay* (IAC role);
> shanahdt MUSI4331; artofcomposing *sentence vs period*. Canonical: Caplin *Classical Form*;
> Schoenberg *Fundamentals*. (Note: two over-specific PAC/IAC framings were REFUTED — the IAC
> is *not* "deferred melodic closure," and the tonic/dominant binary cadence taxonomy failed.)

## 5. TENSION & RELEASE / NON-CHORD TONES  `[C — gap area; partial V]`

`[V]` **Tendency-tone resolution:** the leading tone `^7→^1` (semitone pull); `[C]` `^4→^3`;
the **tritone** (`^7+^4`) resolves outward/inward to `^1+^3`. Dissonance is **the engine of
forward motion** — the lean that *resolves* is what the user calls "emotional weight," and it
is theory, not feel.

`[C]` **The NCT catalog (preparation → dissonance → resolution):**
- **Passing tone** — steps between two chord tones (fills a 3rd). Unaccented usually.
- **Neighbor tone** — steps away and back (upper/lower). Decorates a held pitch.
- **Suspension** — chord tone is **held** (prepared) into the next chord where it becomes a
  dissonance, then **resolves DOWN by step**, on the beat (4-3, 7-6, 9-8, 2-3 bass). The
  strongest "ache + release."
- **Appoggiatura** — **leapt to** on the beat (accented dissonance), **resolves down by step**.
  No preparation → sharper lean than a suspension. (E.g. a ♭7 leapt to over the tonic, falling
  to the 6th.)
- **Anticipation** — a chord tone arrives **early**, before its chord (rhythmic tension).
- **Escape tone (échappée)** — step away, then leap in the opposite direction.
- **Pedal tone** — a sustained pitch (usually tonic/dominant) under changing harmony.

**Recipe:** put a dissonant lean (suspension/appoggiatura) on the strong beat at the point of
greatest expressive weight and resolve it down by step — that is where a phrase *aches*. The
"weight" of a climax can be a prepared `^2-^1` or a suspension, **not** a higher pitch (§6).

> Sources: viva.pressbooks OMT *embellishing tones*; milnepublishing *nonharmonic tones*.

## 6. CLIMAX / APEX, CONTOUR & THE DEEP LINE

`[V, 3-0]` **The single-apex principle:** a strong line has **exactly one climax (high point),
used ONCE**, ideally **leapt to**, falling on a **strong (metric) beat**, and **NOT at the
phrase end**. Overall **range ≤ a 10th, usually under an octave.** (Repeating the high note
"creates the desire to hear the next higher pitch" — it undermines closure.)

`[V, 3-0]` **The arch** is the canonical contour archetype (Huron, *Melodic Arch in Western
Folksongs*, 1996): climb from the lowest note to the apex near the midpoint, then descend.
Other archetypes: terraced descent, gap-fill.

`[V]` **Deep line (Schenker, §0):** the apex and the structural tones belong to the Urlinie;
the rest is prolongation. **Sketch the deep `^3-^2-^1` (or `^5-…-^1`) descent first, then
elaborate.**

`[S — the user's correction, now grounded]` **Emotional weight ≠ pitch height.** The apex is
the line's *structural focal point*, but the moment of greatest **weight** is the prepared
*resolution* (a cadential `^2-^1`, a suspension's release) — which need not be the highest
note. A passing high note (e.g. an early B5 outburst) can sit *below* the climax in weight
while sitting *above* it in pitch.

> Sources: openmusictheory cantusFirmus; OMT species; IBMT; Ableton contour; Huron 1996.

## 7. RHYTHM, METER, TIMING & ANTICIPATION  `[C — gap area; partial V]`

- `[V]` **Structural pitches on strong beats:** the apex and chord tones land on metric
  accents; harmonic rhythm (rate of chord change) usually aligns with strong beats.
- `[C]` **Agogic accent:** a *longer* note is heard as stressed regardless of meter — a long
  note marks a structural pitch even off the beat.
- `[C]` **Timing devices = early vs. late arrival of a structural pitch:**
  - **Anticipation** — the goal arrives *early* (before its beat/chord): eager, forward-pulling.
  - **Suspension** — the goal arrives *late* (the old note is held over): aching delay.
  - **Syncopation** — accent displaced off the metric grid (a note tied across the beat/bar).
- `[C]` **Hemiola** — regrouping (e.g. two bars of 3 heard as three bars of 2), classic at
  cadences.
- `[S]` **Align vs. deliberately misalign:** put structural pitches *on* the beat for stability;
  *displace* them (syncopation/anticipation) for momentum and to carry a phrase across the
  barline (so it doesn't park on every downbeat — §4 "avoiding squareness"). Deploy with
  judgment, **not** stamped uniformly (a constant device becomes the new metronome and reads
  as wandering again).

> Sources: viva.pressbooks OMT *rhythm and meter*; openmusictheory *harmonic syntax*.

## 8. THE CONSTRUCT-&-CRITIQUE CHECKLIST (operational — the anti-doodle)

Run this to BUILD a line and to DIAGNOSE one that wanders:

1. **Deep line** — sing the skeletal `^3-^2-^1` / `^5-…-^1` descent. None present → it wanders. (§0,6)
2. **Tonic frame** — begins on a tonic-triad tone, ends `^2-^1` or `^7-^1` by step. (§1)
3. **One apex** — single high point, once, leapt-to, on a strong beat, not at the phrase end; range ≤ 10th. (§6)
4. **Steps + recovered leaps** — mostly stepwise; leaps balanced by opposite-direction steps; no aug-2/tritone/7th leaps unless the **mode owns them** (villain). (§1,2)
5. **Implied harmony** — chord tones on strong beats; the line outlines a coherent T-PD-D-T / descending-5ths progression; if static, make the *harmony* travel, not the notes. (§3)
6. **Cadence plan** — phrases don't all close hard: weak/open mid-points (HC/IAC), the PAC saved for the end; vary length, use elision/cross-bar ties so it breathes. (§4)
7. **Weight by dissonance** — the expressive peak is a prepared lean (suspension/appoggiatura) resolving down by step on a strong beat — not merely the highest note. (§5,6)
8. **Timing with intent** — structural pitches placed on/against the beat (agogic, anticipation, syncopation) *purposefully and varied*, never one device stamped throughout. (§7)

**Application by affect (archetypes, not prescriptions):**
- **Lyric / love (major, tonal):** a `^3`-line (e.g. `^3-^2-^1`), IAC/HC at the midpoint and
  the PAC at the end; one rationed leapt-to apex; expressive weight from suspensions/
  appoggiaturas resolving down; let the harmony drift to a borrowed ♭VI mixture under a stable
  tune rather than adding melodic motion.
- **Villain / menace (double-harmonic):** *feature* the two aug-2nds and the tritone as
  signature (consonant-by-idiom); downward melodic gravity; Phrygian half-cadences; the menace
  is harmonic stasis + a tendency-tone/tritone shock, NOT constant busy motion (the doodle trap).
- **Battle / drive (minor):** harmonic-minor `^6-^7` aug-2nd available; rising
  circle-of-fifths / sequence to gain energy; structural pitches driven onto strong beats.
