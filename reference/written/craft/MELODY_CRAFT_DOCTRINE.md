# MELODY CRAFT DOCTRINE — distilled lessons (STANCE, not law)

Everything here is *judgment*, recorded so the model writes better single-voice melodies.
None of it is a gate or a hard rule (standing user constraint: "don't encode any laws").
These lessons each surface a deeper layer of the same failure: **the "stencil" instinct —
varying surface detail while holding a deeper structural layer constant.** A good melody
varies on every layer at once.

> **The theory beneath this stance:** `reference/written/craft/melody_theory_foundations.md` — the
> verified music-theory craft (Fux line rules + gap-fill, Schenkerian deep line, cadence
> hierarchy/IAC, single-apex/arch, the double-harmonic aug-2nd idiom, NCT tension/release,
> timing). Use its §8 construct-&-critique checklist as the note-level diagnostic for
> "wandering vs. singing." This doctrine is the *stance*; the melody-theory-foundations doc is the *theory*.

## THE META-FAILURE: stencilling

The recurring root cause. Given a critique, the model would fix the named surface and
keep the deeper structure identical:
- told "add phrasing" → kept the same contour.
- told "vary the contour" → kept writing scalar runs (same construction grammar).
- told "give it a rhythmic core" → stamped ONE rhythmic cell into every bar (a stencil
  one level up).
- told "lock it" → made a few tweaks to the original instead of composing.
The fix is never to stamp the new requirement uniformly. **State an idea, then DEPART
from it, land it, surprise.** A melody that repeats one cell eight times is not a melody.

## THE FOUR INDEPENDENT IDENTITY LAYERS

A theme has several identities, and they must each be distinct and each develop. The
model had been holding most of them constant:
1. **Contour / silhouette** — the shape of the line over the whole phrase. Not every theme
   is the same arch peaking in the middle. Give each a distinct visual shape (launch,
   ascending staircase, double-arch, terraced descent, etc.).
2. **Construction grammar** — *how notes connect*. Step vs leap vs repeated-note-with-accent
   vs alternating-long-with-triad vs sigh vs lurch. The default failure is "scales with
   pauses, up and down." Mix grammars: repeated-note drive, heroic leaps, stepwise
   sequence, syncopated suspensions, broad held arrivals.
3. **Rhythmic core** — a distinct, *clappable* cell that is the theme's signature — BUT
   stated at structural anchors and developed, NOT stencilled into every bar. Catchy
   melodies have a rhythmic identity stronger than their pitch identity.
4. **Harmonic resolution / cadence** — the implied harmonic journey and the LANDINGS.
   Real periodic cadences (half-cadence question → authentic-cadence answer), not a line
   that just stops. This was the single biggest thing missing for the longest time.

## PHRASING

- **Downbeats present.** Bar 1 beat 1 (and structural bars) need a note on the "1". A
  melody that omits the downbeat of bar 1 or 4 "just isn't going to work."
- **Rests on off-beats only**, as punctuation/accent — not on strong beats.
- **Syncopation = a note held across a strong beat** (begins off the beat, suspends over
  the next strong beat) — tension against the barline, not merely a rest.
- **Complexity must match the thematic content** — not uniform syncopation everywhere.
  "Everything needs sufficient complexity in varying types suitable for the content."
  Some themes want stillness, proclamation, or a simple line to declare a theme.

## RHYTHM IS THEORY, NOT FREE DURATION

The deepest rhythmic failure, and the hardest to see: **applying no theory to note-length.**
The model would pick durations to satisfy a *metric* — "traverse the value classes," "no two
bars the same," make the bar sum to 4 — choosing `0.5` here and a dotted `0.75` there with no
musical reason. That is to rhythm exactly what a **scale or an étude is to pitch**: every value
present, nothing *meant*. The user's words: *"that's not rhythm. you're just placing notes of
arbitrary length… aiming to satisfy a metric."* The tells are the same as a doodling pitch
line — no bounce, no phrasing, no hook — because there is no felt pulse for any of those to
live on. Theory beneath this: the melody-theory-foundations doc §7 (metric vs. agogic accent, structural pitches on
strong beats).

The fix is to make **every duration a decision about the meter:**
- **The metric grid is real and fixed.** In 4/4: beat 1 strongest, **beat 3** the secondary
  strong beat, 2 and 4 weak, the "&"s weakest. Every length choice is really *"what lands on a
  strong beat, and what is merely passing through?"* If you can't say which a note is, it's arbitrary.
- **HOLD to weight (agogic accent).** A long note makes whatever it lands on *the event*. So
  the structurally important pitch — the modal shock, the apex, the soured interval — is the
  **long note on a strong beat**, not one more equal value flickering past. (A soured tritone
  held as a quarter on the beat makes its wrongness *ring*; flickered past, it vanishes.)
- **PAUSE to drive energy.** A rest before a downbeat makes that downbeat hit; a rest after a
  gesture makes the listener lean in. **Silence is an accent**, not the filler you reach for to
  make a bar add up. Decide *when to hold and when to pause to drive energy* — that IS rhythm.
- **Pickups are subordinate, so they're short.** Notes driving *up to* the event (a rising
  run into a soured arrival pitch) are fast and light *because they are not the event.* Length
  encodes rank.
- **Variation is structured, not stamped and not random.** A phrase is a rhythmic *argument*:
  state an idea → answer it with a **contrasting** rhythm (the held call vs. the jabbing sneer)
  → develop (compress the holds as energy builds) → broaden at the cadence. One groove stamped
  across the whole theme is as dead as a new value every bar — the first has no phrasing, the
  second no identity. **Vary the rhythm at least once per phrase, usually more.**

Diagnostic: go note by note and state *why each is the length it is* (its metric function). If
the answer for any note is "to fill the bar" or "for variety," it's a rhythmic doodle — rewrite it.

## HARMONY IS PART OF THE LINE, NOT A LATER STEP

The harmonic twin of the rhythm lesson. A single line **already is** a chord progression:
the strong-beat / long notes are heard as chord tones, the rest decorate (passing/neighbor).
Writing a melody with the harmony "pending" — never committing the progression its strong
beats spell — is exactly *"placing notes aiming to satisfy a metric"* one axis over: the line
has no harmonic skeleton to give its accents meaning, so it reads **flat** no matter how the
surface is varied. A theme left with its harmonization "provisional/pending" is the classic
cause of that flatness — the strong beats spell nothing, so they carry no weight.

The fix: **a theme is not finished until its strong-beat skeleton spells a progression you can
name.** Two working orders (melody-theory-foundations §3): *progression-first* (sketch T–PD–D–T, then
place chord tones on strong beats) or *melody-first* (write the line free → lay a progression under
it → nudge the structural tones onto chord tones, the displaced pitches becoming NCTs → smooth the
voice-leading). Default: the downbeat lands the **root**; a strong beat on the 3rd/5th/7th is an
*intentional* mislead/color, not an accident. The payoff is that a repeated cell **travels** —
the same shape recolored by a new chord each pass — instead of restating flat. Every theme
DECLARES the progression it exposes; this is craft/judgment, not a gate.

## SEAMS / PHRASE-ENDS

Cadences and phrase-ends are where energy tends to go to die: long held notes and full-bar
rests. Fix every seam with the SAME move — **keep momentum THROUGH the seam:**
1. **Approach** the high / cadential note with a run (e.g. a triplet anacrusis); don't
   just state it.
2. **Keep the breath SHORT** (e.g. a half-beat rest, not a full beat) — a breath, not dead air.
3. **Hand off with a pickup** across section boundaries, so phrases connect rather than stop.
4. **Adapt the head's pickup to its dramatic position** — e.g. calm repeated-note pickup at
   the opening, rising scalar pickup at the climax. The motif's approach changes with the moment.
5. **Cadences stay rhythmic** — a dotted snap on the 3–2, ti–do with the tonic re-struck,
   rather than one inert held note. A landing can still lift, hand off, and have rhythm.

## RANGE / SINGABILITY

Aim for roughly **one octave to an octave-and-a-half**. It keeps the line singable by an
average human AND makes it far easier to compose variations within. A theme that climbs to
~2.5 octaves is out of comfortable range and hard to develop. When a climactic gesture sits
too high, drop it an octave and add a transition down into it — keeping the climactic shape
(and any signature interval) but in a singable register. (Judgment, not a rule —
proclamation/special moments can break range deliberately.)

## THE WHOLE IS THE UNIT OF ANALYSIS

- **Don't over-reduce to a cell.** A theme is 8–16 bars of often *irreducible* content. The
  power comes from pitch + chord progression + melodic resolution as much as (or more than)
  any short motivic cell. An over-reduced theme loses its memorability, which lives in the
  whole arc, not a four-note tag.
- **A real second idea.** The back half / bridge should be genuinely new material with its
  own contour and rhythm, not the first idea transposed.
- Power often comes from a **harmonic or modal SHOCK** (a single striking interval/chord),
  not from busyness. Constraint is a tool. The lead earns tension from the engine denying it.

## CHROMATIC VARIANTS, SECTIONAL HARMONY & REAL SURPRISE

When a mode supplies more than one chromatic candidate around a single scale region, separate
two distinct devices that can live there and keep them straight:

- **A constant chromatic colour-tone.** One non-scale pitch (e.g. a tritone the head lands on)
  can be the fixed "soured" identity — present in every bar, never swapped, the signature.
- **A variable scale degree.** A *different* degree can have two in-scale forms (e.g. a natural
  vs. a raised version of the same step). Because both belong to the mode, swapping between them
  **recolours** the line rather than sounding wrong. (Don't conflate the two: the fixed
  colour-tone and the swappable degree are different pitches with different jobs.)

Three linked lessons about the variable degree:

1. **Segregate the two forms of the degree by section; do not mix them in a bar.** Let one form
   carry the head/first idea and quarantine the other to the back-half cadences. They then read
   as **two distinct harmonizations / key-colours**, not a wrong note. Throwing both forms into
   the same bar at random just sounds wrong. The sanctioned exception is a *deliberate* half-step
   move at the cadence (the lower form stepping up to the higher), which reads as motion, not error.
2. **Distinct sectional harmonizations buy two things.** (a) Later harmonization gets far
   easier — each region already implies its own chord-world; it's *"not a chord progression,
   but distinct harmonizations."* (b) They enable **thematic repetition with a surprise**.
3. **Real surprise = a thematic return with ONE expected pitch altered — not a random odd
   note.** The pop-psych "earworm + a surprise" is vague; the craft version is exact. Establish
   the expectation by repetition (one form carries the gesture every time), then on the return
   *substitute* the other form so the familiar gesture is recoloured — "a whole new movement."
   The listener expects the old pitch and it isn't there; **that absence is the surprise** — the
   theory-grounded replacement for the "one weird note" cliché.

## REGISTER-CODE A PITCH BY FUNCTION

The same pitch in two octaves can carry different structural roles, assigned by metric
placement. A line can let the **low tonic land** (every cadence resolves there) while the
**upper tonic only ever punctuates — on off-beats, never on a strong-beat arrival.** Keeping the
high tonic as an off-beat flick denies the line a triumphant high-tonic resolution — exactly
right where the affect calls for tension withheld (e.g. menace never released up top).
Generalize: decide each register's *job*, then let metric position enforce it.

## CARDINAL META-LESSONS (how to take direction)

- The user gives **examples to expand creativity, not specs to implement literally.** When
  given an example (e.g. a call-and-response form), do a web search and produce *parallel*
  examples that fit the current movement — don't go hyper-specific on the one example and
  close off everything else. (E8: "third flavor.")
- **A question is a question (E10).** When the user asks "is that intentional?", answer it —
  do NOT silently recompose. Don't treat a complexity example as a formula to stamp.
- When a gate/law fights the music: **raise craft → escalate → never silently lower the bar.**

## A SIGNATURE INTERVAL AS A TWO-NOTE FINGERPRINT

A single striking interval can identify a theme in two notes — a wide consonant leap (e.g. a
major 7th, an octave displacement of a smaller motif) placed at the climax, in singable
register, so the gesture *names* the theme without a full motivic tag. Used sparingly and
landed on a strong beat, it does the identifying work a long cell otherwise would.

A related discipline: **verify claims about a theme against the actual notes, not the design
notes.** A design document may assert an interval or fingerprint the realized line never
contained — that is an idea to add, not an obligation that was dropped. Read the source of
truth (the score/code), not the prose about it.
