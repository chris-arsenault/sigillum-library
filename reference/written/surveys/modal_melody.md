# Survey: the modal melody (a single line built on a non-major/minor mode)

Research capture for the `MEL6_MODAL` library card. The brief: ONE melodic line that is built on
and EXPOSES a modal progression — a mode other than common-practice major/minor. This survey
researches modal melody broadly, then commits to **Dorian** as the chosen mode (rationale below),
captured at full richness so the card is composed FROM this, not from memory.

Provenance: web research run, June 2026. Sources listed at the foot. Companion to
`reference/written/craft/melody_theory_foundations.md` §2 (modal exceptions) & §3 (harmonic
implication) and `MELODY_CRAFT_DOCTRINE.md` ("HARMONY IS PART OF THE LINE").

---

## 0. What "modal" means for a melody (and the central problem)

A mode is a diatonic scale with a tonic OTHER than do/la — so its half-steps fall in different
places, giving each mode a characteristic color note that common-practice major/minor lacks. The
modes (rotations of the major scale), brightest → darkest:

| mode | formula (vs major) | character note | quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lydian | 1 2 3 **#4** 5 6 7 | raised 4th | major |
| Ionian (major) | 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 | — | major |
| Mixolydian | 1 2 3 4 5 6 **♭7** | flat 7th | major |
| **Dorian** | 1 2 **♭3** 4 5 **6** **♭7** | **major 6th over minor 3rd** | minor |
| Aeolian (nat. minor) | 1 2 ♭3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7 | — | minor |
| Phrygian | 1 **♭2** ♭3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7 | flat 2nd | minor |
| Locrian | 1 ♭2 ♭3 4 **♭5** ♭6 ♭7 | flat 5th | dim |

**The central melodic problem of modal writing** (theory-foundations §0/§1, restated for modes): a
diatonic mode shares all seven pitches with TWO familiar tonal scales — its parent major and its
relative natural minor. So a modal line is constantly at risk of being heard NOT in its mode but
in one of those, the moment its strong beats drift toward the wrong tonic. **The whole craft of a
modal melody is keeping the mode audible:** (1) plant the modal tonic as the gravitational centre
(begin/end/cadence there); (2) put the **characteristic degree on strong beats and long values**
so the ear registers the color; (3) AVOID the common-practice leading-tone V→i cadence — most
modes have a ♭7, so there is no raised leading tone to pull to the tonic; the cadence is modal/
plagal/stepwise instead. A modal melody that cadences with a raised leading tone instantly stops
being modal — it has borrowed harmonic-minor and collapsed to tonal.

## 1. Why DORIAN (the chosen mode)

Three reasons, all from the research:

1. **It is the richest single-color modal problem.** Dorian = natural minor with ONE note raised:
   the **major 6th** (`♮6`). That one note is the entire identity. It also keeps the **♭7** (no
   leading tone). So the line lives between two pulls — the ♮6 brightens it AWAY from Aeolian, and
   the ♭7 keeps it AWAY from melodic-minor/major. Holding BOTH audible in one line is the exact
   "keep the mode" craft above, distilled to a single degree. Wikipedia: the major sixth "prevents
   listeners from hearing the relative major as the tonal center, while the lowered seventh
   prevents Aeolian interpretation."
2. **It has the cleanest signature progression** — the **i–IV vamp** (minor tonic → MAJOR four).
   That IV is only major BECASE the 6th is raised (in Aeolian the IV would be minor, iv). So the
   chord progression and the melody declare the mode TOGETHER: the moment the line lands the ♮6 on
   a strong beat over the IV, "this will confirm the Dorian mode" (learningmusicskills). This is a
   textbook "single line exposes its progression" card — the structural craft of the whole library.
3. **It is a distinct color from the nearest existing modal card.** The existing Hijaz /
   Phrygian-dominant material lives on a ♭2 and aug-2nds (dark, exotic, dance). Dorian is the
   bittersweet, "darkness with a hint of light," lyric-folk color — Scarborough Fair, not a Hijaz
   whirl. Genuinely emergent variety (the procedure's mandate) comes from this being a different
   mode researched in its own right.

### D... or which key? — chosen: **E Dorian**
D Dorian is the textbook white-note example (good for *teaching* the scale) but the card should
not default to it. **E Dorian** = `E F# G A B C# D` (relative to D major / parent). Its ♮6 is
**C#** — the bright note. Its ♭7 is **D♮** (NOT D#). Chosen because: E Dorian sits beautifully in
the violin/strings register (open E, A, D, G strings all in the scale; the C# falls on the A
string), it is the key Simon & Garfunkel used for Scarborough Fair, and it keeps the card clearly
distinct in key-centre from the D-tonic Hijaz card.

E Dorian scale, with degree functions:
```
E(1)  F#(2)  G(♭3)  A(4)  B(5)  C#(♮6 ← signature)  D(♭7)  | E(8)
```

## 2. Diatonic chords of E Dorian — and the ones that MATTER

Triads on each degree of E Dorian (`E F# G A B C# D`):

| deg | triad | roman | notes | role for the card |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Em | **i** | E G B | tonic — the gravity well |
| 2 | F#m | ii | F# A C# | contains the ♮6 (C#); modal supertonic (minor, not dim) |
| ♭3 | G | ♭III | G B D | bright relative-major chord — must NOT be allowed to become the tonic |
| 4 | **A** | **IV** | A C# E | **THE Dorian chord** — MAJOR, and it is major only because C# (♮6) is raised |
| 5 | Bm | v | B D F# | MINOR dominant (♭7 in it = D); the **anti-leading-tone** — no V→i pull |
| 6 | C#dim | vi° | C# E G | diminished |
| ♭7 | D | ♭VII | D F# A | the subtonic major — strong modal cadence chord (♭VII→i) |

**The two load-bearing facts:**
- The **IV (A major)** and the **ii (F#m)** are the only diatonic chords that CONTAIN the
  signature C#. Landing the line's C# on a strong beat over A major is the single clearest "this is
  Dorian" event. (learningmusicskills: emphasize the ♮6, "especially when played over the IV chord.")
- The **v is minor (Bm), and ♭VII (D) is major** — so the cadence to i is NOT a leading-tone
  authentic cadence. A Dorian cadence is **♭VII→i (D→Em)** or **IV→i (A→Em, a modal plagal)** or a
  stepwise ♭7→1 / ♮6→5 melodic close. Using a B *major* (raised D→D#) would import a leading tone
  and destroy the mode — forbidden here.

### Chosen progression the strong beats will expose (committed, not pending)
A two-cell Dorian design, period-shaped (antecedent answers open, consequent closes):
```
b1  Em   (i)      b5  Em   (i)
b2  A    (IV)     b6  D    (♭VII)
b3  G    (♭III)   b7  A    (IV)
b4  D    (♭VII)   b8  Em   (i)
```
- The **i–IV** pair (b1→b2, b7) is the signature vamp; the ♮6 (C#) sits on the IV's strong beats.
- **♭III (G)** at b3 is the "hint of light" pull toward the relative major — deliberately touched
  then pulled back, not landed as a tonic.
- **♭VII (D)** at b4 and b6 is the modal cadence chord; b4 makes a modal half-close (open), b8
  closes ♭VII→i / a stepwise ♭7→1 Dorian PAC.
- No B major anywhere — the v stays implied-minor; the close is modal, never leading-tone.

## 3. How a single line EXPOSES Dorian (the mechanism, note-level)

From theory-foundations §3 + the modal sources:
- **Strong-beat skeleton = chord tones of the bar's chord** (downbeat usually the root). Decorations
  (passing/neighbor/escape) sit on weak parts and resolve by step.
- **The signature degree is given AGOGIC weight:** the C# (♮6) is not a flicker — at least once it
  is a HELD note on a strong beat over A major, so its brightness rings. This is the "hold to weight"
  rhythm lesson (doctrine §RHYTHM): the structurally meaningful pitch is the long note on the beat.
- **The ♭7 (D♮) is featured, not corrected to D#:** wherever the line nears the tonic from below it
  steps D→E (♭7→1, a whole step, soft) rather than the half-step leading-tone pull. This whole-step
  approach is itself an audible "this is modal" tell.
- **Avoid the relative-major trap:** the line touches G (♭III) and its tone D, but never lets G sit
  on a strong downbeat as a goal; E gravity is re-asserted immediately after.

## 4. Repertoire (concrete, across idioms) — the WHY per item

- **"Scarborough Fair" (English trad., S&G in A Dorian)** — THE canonical Dorian folk tune. Modal
  ballad: minor 3rd, major 6th, minor 7th; the melody leans on the ♮6 and cadences modally, not
  with a leading tone. Lyric, spacious, archaic. (en.wikipedia Scarborough Fair; hooktheory.)
- **"Drunken Sailor" (sea shanty, D Dorian)** — the i–♭VII oscillation; a brisk modal work-song;
  shows Dorian can be propulsive, not only mournful.
- **Miles Davis "So What" / Herbie Hancock "Maiden Voyage" (D Dorian)** — the founding modal-jazz
  vamp: long stretches over ONE Dorian chord, the melody/improv free to roam the mode; the ♮6 is
  the color that keeps it from being plain minor. (en.wikipedia Dorian, Modal jazz.)
- **Beatles "Eleanor Rigby" (E Dorian / E Aeolian mixture)** — Em↔C with a C# coloring; pop proof
  the mode reads as bittersweet, not exotic.
- **"Mad World" (Tears for Fears), "Get Lucky" (Daft Punk), "About Damn Time" (Lizzo)** — modern
  i–IV / i–♭VII Dorian vamps; the major IV's "lift and forward momentum" is the hook.
- **Koji Kondo "Song of Storms" (Zelda, D Dorian-ish)** — game-music Dorian: a whirling minor tune
  brightened by the ♮6; proof the mode carries a memorable HOOK, not just atmosphere.
- **Plainchant "Dies irae," "Ave maris stella" (mode I, Dorian)** — the medieval origin; stepwise,
  reciting-tone-centred, modally cadenced — the ancestor of the "no leading tone" close.

**Character consensus:** Dorian is "darkness with a hint of light" (composercode) — minor-grounded
but the ♮6 keeps it from being lugubrious. Equally at home lyric-mournful (Scarborough Fair) or
brisk-folk (Drunken Sailor). The card takes the **brisk-folk, dancing** reading — a modal jig — so
it is not a dirge, and contrasts the lyric cards already in the set.

## 5. The card's design brief (derived from all the above)

- **Mode/key:** E Dorian (`E F# G A B C# D`), tonic E. Signature ♮6 = C#; modal ♭7 = D♮.
- **Meter/tempo:** **6/8, brisk** (a modal folk jig — ~132 dotted-quarter feel). 6/8 gives the
  lilting compound swing of Drunken Sailor / a reel; brisk so it DANCES, not a dirge (doctrine: no
  60bpm recitals). beats=3.0 (six eighths = 2 dotted-quarter beats summing to 3.0 quarter-lengths).
- **Character:** brisk bittersweet modal folk-dance — bright ♮6 lift inside a minor frame.
- **Contour:** a launched arch — open low on the tonic, climb across the antecedent to a leapt-to
  apex (the ♮6-colored peak) early in the consequent (b5), terraced descent to a modal close.
- **Construction grammar mix:** compound-time arpeggio lift, a repeated-note drive, a stepwise
  ♮6-leaning sigh, a leap to the apex, broad held cadence — NOT one cell stamped (anti-stencil).
- **The HOOK:** a two-note ♮6→5 "brightening" sigh (C#→B) that recurs as the signature, landed
  over the A-major IV so the major-6th-over-minor color rings. Stated, departed from, returned.
- **Rests load-bearing:** compound-time gives natural breath at the end of each dotted-quarter
  group; a clear breath before the apex (b5) and before the cadence; not wall-to-wall eighths.
- **Cadence plan:** modal half-close (open) at b4 on ♭VII (D), the consequent closes ♭VII→i with a
  stepwise ♭7→1 (D→E) Dorian PAC — NO leading tone (no D#).
- **Apex:** ONE — C#5 region, leapt to, on a strong beat at b5, held (agogic), not at the phrase end.
- **Range:** ~an octave + a bit, singable, violin's warm middle (E4–F#5 region).
