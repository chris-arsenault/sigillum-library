# Ground Bass / Passacaglia as a CUMULATIVE TEXTURAL ENGINE (survey, NOT distilled)

The technique: **a short bass line (or harmonic ground) repeats unchanged, and the music above it
accretes — variation by variation the upper texture thickens, the rhythm subdivides, the register
climbs — so that intensity is built by ACCUMULATION OVER A FIXED FOUNDATION rather than by going
somewhere new harmonically.** The ground is the constant; everything audibly *grows* against its
constancy. The ear hears the fixed loop as a measuring stick, so each added layer reads as a gain.
This is the engine: not "a repeating bass" as decoration, but repetition-as-architecture, where the
floor's refusal to move is precisely what makes the rising upper voices feel like a crescendo of
*structure*. Pitches scientific, middle C = C4. Flats spelled "B-" = B♭ etc.

This survey is BOTH the source the card is composed from AND a durable library reference. Detail is
the point; it is deliberately not distilled into a checklist.

## 1. The names (a HIERARCHY of overlapping terms — they differ on scope and on bass-voice fixity)

The terms nest. Open Music Theory and Wikipedia agree on the containment:

> Every chaconne/passacaglia is also a ground bass; every ground bass is also an ostinato — but not
> every ostinato is a ground bass, and not every ground bass is a chaconne/passacaglia.

- **OSTINATO** (Italian "obstinate") — the broadest: ANY pattern (melodic, rhythmic, or harmonic)
  that repeats persistently in ONE voice through a long section or whole work. It need not be in the
  bass (a repeated rhythmic cell in the timpani is an ostinato; a riff in the guitar is an ostinato).
  The repeating thing is the only requirement.
- **GROUND BASS / BASSO OSTINATO / "the ground"** — the SPECIFIC case where the repeating ostinato
  is *the bass line*, repeated "usually exactly, or nearly so," throughout a piece or section, with
  the variety supplied by the parts ABOVE it. Highly popular in the Baroque; still ubiquitous in pop.
  English usage: "divisions on a ground" = variations played over the ground.
- **CHACONNE (ciaccona)** — a Baroque ground-bass subgenre: continuous variation, slow TRIPLE metre,
  serious character. Origin: a fast, sexually suggestive dance-song from the Spanish New World (early
  17th c.), tamed into the noble instrumental form. In its purest theoretic form the chaconne is built
  on a repeating *harmonic* progression / bass *pattern* (the bass can be reornamented) rather than a
  literally fixed melodic bass; early ciaccona was characterised by a bass motion roughly I–V–vi–I.
- **PASSACAGLIA (passacaille)** — the close cousin: also Baroque, triple metre, serious. Origin: a
  Spanish guitarists' *pasacalle* ("walk the street") ritornello strummed between song-verses. Early
  passacaglia leaned to a STEPWISE DESCENDING bass (e.g. i – V6 – VI – V), often the descending
  tetrachord. The textbook distinction asserted in many guides:
  - **chaconne** = the repeating element is the HARMONIC progression / bass *pattern* (may migrate,
    may be reornamented); variations over a chord scheme.
  - **passacaglia** = the repeating element is a fixed bass *melody/theme* that can be transferred to
    OTHER voices (Brahms's finale theme appears in the treble, in inner voices, in the bass).
  **CRUCIAL CAVEAT (every serious source repeats it):** "composers used the terms chaconne and
  passacaglia indiscriminately, and modern attempts to arrive at a clear distinction are arbitrary
  and historically unfounded." Bach's BWV 1004 *Chaconne* fits the "passacaglia" definition; the
  reverse mislabellings abound. Treat the chaconne/passacaglia split as a soft tendency, not a law.
- **LAMENT BASS / passus duriusculus / descending (chromatic) tetrachord** — the single most
  important *specific* ground: a stepwise bass descent of a perfect fourth from tonic to dominant.
  Diatonic minor form and a fully chromatic form (see §3). Carries a death/grief topos.
- **ROMANESCA / standing schemata** — Gjerdingen's galant schemata (Romanesca, Folia, Passamezzo)
  are recurring bass+harmony moulds; Pachelbel's Canon ground is a Romanesca descent. These are
  "grounds" in the loose sense — recurring foundational patterns that breed variation.
- **GROUND in pop/rock = "the loop / the vamp / the riff-based form"** — a 2-, 4-, or 8-bar
  chord-loop or bassline repeated for the whole track while the arrangement accumulates (a verse adds
  a hi-hat, the chorus adds the full band, the bridge strips back). The same engine, renamed.
- **VAMP (jazz/gospel/musical theatre)** — a short repeated chord/bass figure held ("vamp till
  ready") under improvised or building material; the gospel "drive"/"special" rides a vamp while the
  choir and organ accrete to ecstasy. Cumulative engine, oral tradition.

**Which name fits THIS card?** The assigned idea — ostinato as a *cumulative textural engine* — is
most precisely the **ground bass / passacaglia** (the bass-fixed, variation-accreting case). I name
the card **GB_GROUND_BASS** and build it on a passacaglia-style descending-tetrachord ground so it
also teaches the lament-bass topos, the single richest ground in the literature.

## 2. The mechanics & the WHY (repetition-as-architecture; how variations accrete)

The WHY, stated by Open Music Theory and the SFCM "Ostinato and Variation" materials: a fixed bass
"creates a sense of stability, allowing upper voices to vary freely, much like a canvas supporting
layers of paint." The brevity of the ground lets the composer "focus on elaboration in upper voices,
building complexity without overwhelming the foundation." Because the floor is a known quantity that
repeats on a fixed clock, the listener's ear treats it as a *reference grid*: every change above it
is measured against it and therefore reads as ACCUMULATION. You don't have to modulate to build —
you add.

The accretion devices, drawn across the sources (Open Music Theory, the Bach BWV 582 analyses,
Lumen "Themes and Variations", the SFCM lecture, the tiboresque structural survey):

1. **Rhythmic diminution / acceleration per statement** — the master device. Statement 1: the ground
   bare or in slow note-values. Then "the pace is accelerated with the movement to sixteenth notes";
   "continuous eighth notes and sixteenth notes" are introduced; "these changes usually coincide with
   the start of a new iteration of the ground." Each new pass subdivides finer — quarters → eighths →
   triplets → sixteenths → thirty-seconds — so motion energy climbs while harmony stays put. Bach
   BWV 582 explicitly: the ostinato is unchanged in the bass for the first variations, then "in the
   fourth the pace is accelerated with the movement to sixteenth notes."
2. **Textural thickening / layer addition** — start thin (bass alone), add one voice, then a full
   contrapuntal web. "Initial variations comprise imitative pairs… building rhythmic and textural
   density from simple homophony to fuller polyphony." Voices ENTER cumulatively; nothing leaves.
3. **Registral ascent** — figuration climbs from the bass octave up into the treble (the Zimmer
   film-ostinato arc: "rising string pulses, starting from low rhythmic figures and building upward
   into violins, the shift in register mirroring tension → struggle → triumph"). Higher = brighter,
   more present, hotter.
4. **Re-harmonisation over the SAME bass** — "inventive composers find ways to re-harmonise the same
   bass line to move to other keys, or at least hint at such a move"; local tonicisations colour
   individual statements without breaking the loop. The bass note is fixed; the chord on top of it
   changes.
5. **Theme migration (the passacaglia move)** — "the most striking change occurs when the bass theme
   is for the first time equally treated in all of the voices" / migrates to the treble. The ground
   leaves the floor and saturates the texture — a structural climax.
6. **Imitation/canon over the ground** — Pachelbel's Canon literally stacks a three-voice canon atop
   the ground; the canon's progressive entries are themselves an accretion engine.
7. **Dynamic & orchestrational growth** — pp bass → mf added voices → f full texture; in the orchestra
   add families, in pop add the drum kit / the band. The loop is the constant against which the
   build is legible.
8. **Grouping into waves** — Bach's BWV 582 is "21 statements in seven groups of three"; builds "to
   its climax in variation twelve," then "three quiet variations forming a short intermezzo" (a
   strategic DROP — the engine includes the release), then five to the end. Cumulative does not mean
   monotonic: a planned thinning resets the ear so the final build lands harder.

The expressive payoffs the sources name: STABILITY + INEVITABILITY (the loop is fate — Dido's death,
the "ticking time bomb" of a film cue); HYPNOSIS (relentless repetition); and a CROWD/ECSTASY arc
(gospel drive, EDM build, the symphonic chaconne's grand accumulation). Schumann on the BWV 582
variations: "intertwined so ingeniously that one can never cease to be amazed."

## 3. The grounds themselves — EXACT NOTES

### 3a. The lament bass / descending tetrachord (the card's chosen ground)
Wikipedia ("Lament bass") gives the forms exactly. Stepwise descent of a perfect fourth, tonic→dominant:

- **Diatonic minor (Phrygian tetrachord):** scale-degrees 8–♭7–♭6–5.
  - A minor: **A – G – F – E**
  - G minor: **G – F – E♭ – D**  (i.e. G – F – E- – D)
  - D minor: **D – C – B♭ – A**  (D – C – B- – A)
- **Chromatic (passus duriusculus — every semitone filled, the "harder passage"):**
  - D minor: **D – C♯ – C♮ – B – B♭ – A**  (D – C# – C – B – B- – A)
  - G minor (Dido): **G – F♯ – F♮ – E♭(or E♮ passing) – D**; Wikipedia gives the Dido descent as
    **G – F♯ – F♮ – E – E♭ – D** then back to G (six notes filling the fourth chromatically).
  - A minor: **A – G♯ – G♮ – F♯ – F♮ – E**

Typical harmonisation of the diatonic minor tetrachord (each bass note carries a chord; this is what
makes it a re-fittable chord-slot ground): **i – v6 (or V/♭III) – ♭VI – V** in minor; in the
Andalusian-cadence reading **i – ♭VII – ♭VI – V** (A minor: Am – G – F – E). The chromatic version
threads applied/diminished chords between (e.g. i – viio7/V – iv6 – … – V), which is what produces
the grinding poignancy.

### 3b. Purcell, "Dido's Lament" (*When I am laid in earth*, Dido and Aeneas, 1689)
- **Ground (G minor, 3/2):** chromatic descending fourth **G – F♯ – F♮ – E♭ – D**, then a cadential
  tail back up to **G** (the full ground is ~5 bars / spans the chromatic fourth + cadence).
- Repeated **eleven times** under the aria; "structuring the piece in the form of a passacaglia."
- WHY it is the canonical engine example: the bass is a fixed chromatic descent = death/agony; the
  vocal line is set so its PHRASES OVERLAP the ground's cadences (the singer's phrase begins before
  the ground resolves, so the seams never align — the voice floats free of the loop's clock). Word-
  painting: "laid" given its own descending chromatic line; "Remember me" repeated, the final cry
  "leaping in register with a sudden crescendo" — the climax is built by the VOICE rising over the
  unmoved ground. Phrase-overlap + registral lift over a fixed bass = the technique in miniature.

### 3c. J.S. Bach, Passacaglia (and Fugue) in C minor, BWV 582 (organ)
- **Ground:** an **8-bar** theme in **3/4**, stated alone "in the pedal with no accompaniment" — deep,
  slow, "weighty iambic." (The theme's first half was likely borrowed from André Raison's organ
  *Christe* trio.) 8 bars of triple-metre ground is "unusual but not unheard of" — and is the warrant
  for an 8-bar card. The bass walks down and around the C-minor tonic; its first half later becomes
  the fugue subject.
- **20 variations** (21 statements counting the bare opening), in **seven groups of three**.
- The ACCRETION, variation by variation (synthesised from the analyses):
  - **Var. 1–4:** ground stays in the bass; treble adds material; "in the fourth the pace accelerates
    with the movement to sixteenth notes" — diminution kicks in.
  - **Var. 5–10:** "increasing contrapuntal and rhythmic complexity," running sixteenths, rising and
    falling figures; texture thickens from homophony to polyphony.
  - **Climax at var. 12.**
  - **Then three quiet variations** — a short **intermezzo** (the strategic DROP).
  - **Later variations:** the theme MIGRATES out of the bass into upper voices ("for the first time
    equally treated in all of the voices"); thirty-second-note runs "create vast space"; the theme
    returns to the bass in diminished form for the close.
- Schumann: the variations are "intertwined so ingeniously one can never cease to be amazed." This is
  the single best didactic model of cumulative-engine craft: same 8-bar floor, twenty escalating
  surfaces, with a built-in release before the final ascent.

### 3d. Pachelbel, Canon in D (Canon and Gigue in D, c.1680–1706)
- **Ground:** an **8-note** bass, **two bars in 4/4**, originally **eight quarter notes**:
  **D – A – B – F♯ – G – D – G – A** (D3 register). Roman numerals **I – V – vi – iii – IV – I – IV – V**
  (a Romanesca-type descent). Repeated ~**28 times**.
- Engine: three violins enter in CANON over the unchanging ground; each canonic voice elaborates in
  progressively finer note-values (half notes → quarters → eighths → sixteenths) as the piece builds,
  then thins back. Pure accretion-by-diminution + canonic layering. The progression became the most
  recycled loop in pop (Vitamin C "Graduation," Green Day "Basket Case," Maroon 5 "Memories," etc.).

### 3e. Brahms, Symphony No. 4 in E minor, finale (1885) — the symphonic passacaglia/chaconne
- **Theme: 8 bars**, stated at the very top of the movement (in the winds, as a rising-then-turning
  chordal idea whose top voice is essentially a stepwise ascent E–F♯–G–A–A♯–B–B–E with the harmony
  beneath). Brahms adapted it from the chaconne bass closing Bach's cantata BWV 150 *Nach dir, Herr,
  verlanget mich*.
- **30 statements**, "always 8 bars long," then a deviation just before the **Più Allegro coda** (bar
  253). A textbook of the passacaglia move: the theme migrates between bass, inner voices and treble;
  variations group into waves of building and release (the famous flute solo variation is a planned
  thinning); the first movement's descending thirds return in counterpoint in a late variation. The
  proof that the cumulative engine scales to a full symphonic movement.
- Lineage Brahms drew on: Buxtehude's *Passacaglia in D minor* BuxWV 161 and chaconnes; Bach.

### 3f. Other classical grounds
- **J.S. Bach, "Crucifixus"** (B-minor Mass) — lament bass, descending chromatic fourth in E minor;
  the choir's grief accretes over the fixed sinking bass; the final "sub Pontio Pilato" lifts off it.
- **Handel, Passacaglia in G minor** (Suite No. 7, HWV 432) — a ground worked into bravura keyboard
  variations of escalating figuration (Halvorsen's famous violin-viola duo arrangement is built on it).
- **Monteverdi, "Lamento della ninfa"** — descending tetrachord A–G–F–E ground under a grieving
  soprano with three commenting male voices; an early dramatic ground.

### 3g. Film / game (the modern cumulative engine)
- **Hans Zimmer** "makes considerable use" of rhythmic/string ostinati. *Inception* — "hypnotic
  rhythmic figures that build tension and momentum like a ticking time bomb." *Dark Knight Rises* —
  "rising string pulses, starting from low rhythmic figures and building upward into violins," register
  mirroring "tension, struggle, triumph." *Interstellar* — ostinati + arpeggiated figures racing,
  with organ bass notes enhancing the climax. *Pirates of the Caribbean* — "a number of different
  ostinati in many of the parts layered to produce the overall sound… a driving rhythmic ostinato that
  builds under percussion and synths, gradually layered with strings." The film method is literally
  "loop a short bass/rhythm figure and ACCUMULATE layers" = the ground-bass engine in scoring.

### 3h. Pop / rock / jazz / gospel
- **The Andalusian cadence as ostinato:** **i – ♭VII – ♭VI – V** = a descending minor tetrachord in
  the bass, used "most often as an ostinato, repeating over and over." A minor: **Am – G – F – E**,
  bass **A – G – F – E**. Ray Charles, "Hit the Road Jack" "is nothing but a repeated Andalusian
  cadence the whole way through" — the arrangement (horns, backing vocals, Charles's vocal) accretes
  over the fixed four-chord loop. Flamenco, Arabic maqām, and countless rock songs share it. (Note the
  identity with the diatonic minor lament tetrachord of §3a — same four bass notes, "sad" topos.)
- **Lament-bass pop:** The Beatles "While My Guitar Gently Weeps," Green Day "Brain Stew," Muse, Led
  Zeppelin "Stairway to Heaven" intro (A – G♯ – G♮ – F♯ chromatic descent) — Wikipedia's own pop list
  for the lament bass.
- **Pachelbel-progression pop** (§3d).
- **Gospel/soul VAMP & DRIVE:** a short chord/bass vamp held while organ, choir, hand-claps and
  ad-libs accrete to an ecstatic peak — the oral-tradition cumulative engine.

## 4. What this tells the card

- The card should be a **ground bass / passacaglia** built on a **descending tetrachord** (teaches the
  single richest ground + the lament topos), with the ground stated bare first, then surfaces accreting
  by **rhythmic diminution + voice addition + registral ascent + one theme-migration**, with the
  passacaglia's signature **strategic drop** acknowledged in shape if 8 bars allow.
- **8 bars** is correct and idiomatic: Bach BWV 582 (8-bar ground) and Brahms 4 (8-bar theme) both use
  exactly 8. To show ACCUMULATION inside 8 bars, the natural reading is a **short ground (≈2 bars) that
  repeats 4 times**, one accretion stage per repeat (bare → +inner voice/diminution → +treble figuration
  → climax/migration), with the final cadence the payoff. This is the Pachelbel/Dido proportion (short
  ground, many repeats) compressed to a card; it lets the FIXED FLOOR repeat audibly four times so the
  build is legible against it — the whole point of the engine.
- **Meter:** triple metre is the historical home (chaconne/passacaglia), so **3/4** is the authentic
  choice and distinguishes this card from the library's many 4/4 cards. (Dido is 3/2, Bach/Brahms 3/4.)
- **Chord-slots:** the four descending-tetrachord bass notes ARE the harmonic skeleton (i – ♭VII –
  ♭VI – V or i – v6 – ♭VI – V), so the card is transposable/re-fittable to any minor key by sliding
  the tetrachord — exactly the parameterisation the card spec wants.
- **Scoring:** strings are the idiom where this engine most commonly LIVES across eras (Baroque ground
  consorts; Brahms's orchestral passacaglia; Zimmer's string ostinati; the lament-bass topos). A
  string ensemble (Vc/Cb ground + Va + Vn2 + Vn1) lets the ground sit in the true bass and the
  accretion climb register through the section — the library already has a CHAMBER STRINGS category to
  match against. Re-scorable to organ/piano (Bach/Handel/Pachelbel) or mixed chamber.
