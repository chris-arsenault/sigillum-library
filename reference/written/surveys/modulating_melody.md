# The Modulating Melody — a single line that carries an audible key change (survey, NOT distilled)

A line that **modulates inside itself**: the key change is hearable from the melody ALONE, before
(or without) any chord confirming it. This is the melodic counterpart of harmonic modulation — the
line itself supplies the signal. Captured from a genuine external research pass (June 2026); preserved
rich, not boiled down to a checklist. Source for `MEL5_MODULATING`.

---

## 0. THE DISTINCTION THAT MATTERS — modulation vs. tonicization (by DURATION)

`[V]` Wikipedia *Tonicization*: "if a chord is treated as the tonic for **longer than a phrase**
before returning to the previous key area, then the treatment is considered a **modulation** to a new
key." Tonicization is a *brief* tip of the hat to a non-tonic as local tonic (under a phrase); a
**modulation** is a *committed, sustained* relocation of the tonal centre — the line stays in the new
key, cadences there, and does not snap back. For an 8-bar card the technique we want is a genuine
**modulation**: the second half LIVES in the new key and CADENCES there (PAC in the new tonic), it
does not merely visit.

## 1. THE CORE MECHANISM — what makes a key-change AUDIBLE FROM ONE LINE

`[V]` Wikipedia *Modulation*: modulation requires **melodic components** — "a recognizable segment of
the scale of the quasi-tonic **or strategically placed leading-tone**." Two things make a single line
read as having modulated, no accompaniment needed:

1. **The NEW LEADING TONE — the load-bearing signal.** Each major key has exactly ONE accidental that
   the old key lacked, and it is the **leading tone (scale-degree 7) of the new key**. Hearing that
   pitch *resolve up a semitone to the new tonic* is the ear's proof the centre has moved.
   - `[V]` Pressbooks *Tonal Modulation I* (rwu): **C major → G major**, the new accidental is **F#**,
     which "is the audible signal of the new key because it functions as scale degree 7 (the leading
     tone) in G major, creating harmonic pull toward the new tonal center." Generalize: **to modulate
     UP a fifth to the dominant, RAISE scale-degree 4** (it becomes #4 = the new ^7) and resolve it up
     to ^5-of-old = ^1-of-new.
   - `[V]` Same source, the mirror: **modulate DOWN a fifth to the subdominant** by **flattening
     scale-degree 7** (B → B♭ in C→F); the ♭7 "is the audible signal," now functioning as ^4 of F.
2. **A recognizable SCALE SEGMENT of the new key** — running the new key's diatonic notes (especially a
   stepwise ^7→^1 or a ^2→^1 close *in the new key*) plants the new tonic by sheer scalar context.

`[V]` *Closely related keys* / *Common tone (scale)*: the **dominant** is the smoothest target —
transposition by a perfect fifth shares **6 of 7 common tones**, so only ONE pitch changes (the #4).
That single chromatic note doing all the work is exactly why a dominant modulation is the cleanest to
hear in a bare line: everything else is shared, the one new sharp is unmistakable.

## 2. THE FAMILY OF MODULATION TYPES (mechanics, from Wikipedia *Modulation* + OMT + Musicnotes)

- **Common-chord / diatonic pivot** `[V]`: pass through a chord shared by both keys, reinterpreted
  (G-major I = D-major IV). The predominants (ii, IV of the new key) are the favored pivots. In a
  single LINE this becomes a **pivot TONE/figure** — a beat whose notes belong to both keys, after
  which the line tilts new.
- **Common-TONE modulation** `[V]` (thinkingmusic; intmus): NO pivot chord — a single pitch is
  **sustained or repeated** and **reinterpreted as a different scale degree** in the new key, then the
  context resolves around it. "These modulations are labeled with pivot brackets, but instead of chords
  we put **scale degrees**." Ideal for thirds-related keys (C↔A♭ share C; C↔E share E). For ONE line
  this is the purest device: **hold a note, let it flip meaning** (e.g. old ^5 = new ^1).
- **Chromatic / direct (phrase) modulation** `[V]`: one phrase cadences in the old key, the next simply
  *begins* in the new — no transition. The pop "**truck driver's gear change**."
- **Sequential** `[V]`: transpose a closing figure up/down to a new level (rosalia) — the descending-5ths
  chain is the engine.
- **Enharmonic** `[V]`: respell a chord (dim7, Ger+6 = dom7) to pivot to a distant key.
- **Chain** `[V]`: reach a far key through close intermediaries (C→G→D).

## 3. THE DOMINANT MODULATION IS THE CLASSICAL DEFAULT — and the cleanest for a bare line

`[V/C]` In sonata/binary practice the first reflex of a major-key piece is to modulate to **the
dominant** (V). Mechanically (Pressbooks): the home key tonicizes V by introducing **#4** (the
secondary leading tone, `vii°/V` or `V/V`), then a cadence in the new key confirms it. As a SINGLE
LINE: state the home tonic, then at the hinge **leap to or hold the #4 on a strong beat** (its alien-ness
is maximal there — it's the one note outside the home scale), **resolve it up by semitone to the new
tonic**, and **cadence ^2→^1 or ^7→^1 in the new key**. The held #4 on a strong beat, framed by silence,
IS the modulation made audible — exactly the doctrine's "structurally important pitch = the long note on
a strong beat" (`MELODY_CRAFT_DOCTRINE.md` §RHYTHM; the Adversary's soured A♭ is the model).

## 4. EMOTIONAL / DRAMATIC FUNCTION (why a melody modulates)

- **UP to the dominant** = **brightening, tension/lift, opening-out** — `[V]` *Dominant*: modulating to
  the dominant *increases tension*; to the subdominant *relaxes*. The up-a-fifth move is the "and now,
  higher / further" gesture: a hero crossing into new country, the second wind.
- **The pop gear-change (up a tone/semitone, late)** `[V]` Musical-U / TV Tropes: a cheap-but-effective
  surge of energy for a final chorus — "motion towards a climax." Musical-U is openly critical ("our
  ears naturally take interest in a key change, but that doesn't equate with goodness") — so the craft
  job is to make the modulation *earned and prepared* (a real new leading-tone pull), not a brute
  semitone bump.
- **Common-tone, thirds-related** = **a sudden new light** — the held note stays while the world around
  it recolors (the cinematic "wonder" modulation).

## 5. CONCRETE REPERTOIRE (across idioms)

- **Classical binary/sonata expositions** — modulate to V (major) or III (minor) as a structural law;
  the melody itself spells the new key via #4 → new-^1.
- **Disney "Can You Feel the Love Tonight"** `[V]` — F → G (up a tone) for the final verses.
- **The Supremes "I Hear a Symphony"** `[V]` — several stacked gear-changes from ~1:10.
- **Macy Gray "I Try"** `[V]` — D major → F major (bridge) → up to E♭ before the final chorus (a
  modulate-then-modulate-again arc).
- **Roxette "Listen to Your Heart"** `[V]` — up a major third. **Live "Run to the Water"** — C→D for the
  coda. **"The Rainbow Connection"** — up a semitone before the last verse.

## 6. HOW TO BUILD A MODULATING LINE (synthesis, applied to the card)

Order of operations for a single 8-bar line that audibly modulates **up a fifth to the dominant**
(the cleanest, classical, one-pitch-changes case):

1. **Halves, not a smear.** First half (≈ b1–4) firmly in the home key: a tonic-triad opening, a
   clear progression on the strong beats (I … V), an open mid-cadence (HC/IAC) so it WANTS to go on.
2. **The hinge is an EVENT, framed by silence.** Put a **rest before** the modulating note — silence is
   an accent (doctrine §7); the breath makes the pivot register as a turn, not a slip.
3. **The #4 is the one chromatic, held on a strong beat.** Introduce the single new accidental (#4 of
   the home key = ^7 of the new key) as a **long agogic note on a downbeat**, leapt to. It is the only
   note outside the home scale — that's why the ear hears the door open.
4. **Resolve it up by semitone to the new tonic**, and **stay** — the second half lives in the new key:
   strong beats spell a progression IN the new key (e.g. new-I … new-V7 … new-I), use a recognizable
   scale segment of the new key, and **CADENCE there** (PAC: new-^7→new-^1 onto a held new tonic, or
   ^2→^1). Because it cadences in the new key for longer than a phrase, it is a MODULATION, not a
   tonicization (§0).
5. **Don't snap back.** A modulating line ENDS in the new key — the whole point is the relocation holds.

**Verification (`production_view SOURCE.rb melody_analysis --part PART`):** the strong-beat skeleton of the first half spells the home progression
with chord tones of the HOME key; after the hinge, the strong beats spell a progression whose roots are
the NEW key's I/IV/V and whose chord tones are NEW-key tones; the single #4 reads as the new ^7 resolving
to new ^1. The line begins on home-^1 and ends on **new-^1**.
