# The I–V–vi–IV "Recolor" Engine — survey (Axis progression as a melodic-anchor machine)

Captured for the melody-card slot `MEL1_POP_IVVIIV`. This is the durable library reference AND
the source the card is composed from. The technique: ONE melodic line travels over the four-chord
Axis cycle while the harmony *recolors beneath it* — the same melody note changes harmonic function
as each chord arrives. Research run: WebSearch + WebFetch, June 2026 (sources at the bottom).

---

## 1. WHAT IT IS — names, definition, rotations

The **I–V–vi–IV progression** (a.k.a. the **"Axis progression"**, popularized as the **"Axis of
Awesome / Four Chords"** after the 2008 comedy medley that stitched dozens of global hits over it,
>100M combined video views by 2020). In C major: **C – G – Am – F**.

It is "the most common progression in modern pop and rock." Part of why: there are few orders to
play four chords starting on I, and these four ARE the four most-used pop chords. But the deeper
reason is harmonic-functional (§2) and melodic (§3).

**The four primary rotations** (same four chords, different start — each a different mood):
- **I–V–vi–IV** — C–G–Am–F. The "optimistic / anthem" rotation. Starts grounded on tonic.
- **V–vi–IV–I** — G–Am–F–C. Starts on the dominant's lift; the I arrival is delayed (payoff at the end).
- **vi–IV–I–V** — Am–F–C–G. The **"sensitive female chord progression"**; starts in the relative
  minor, reads *melancholy → brightening*. Wikipedia: this rotation "modulates the key to A minor"
  perceptually while staying inside C major's diatonic set — the **double-tonic ambiguity** that is
  the engine's whole emotional duality.
- **IV–I–V–vi** — F–C–G–Am. Starts on the plagal lift, ends on the deceptive minor.

**Distinct neighbors (do NOT confuse):**
- **'50s progression** = I–vi–IV–V (C–Am–F–G) — older, "doo-wop," a different animal.
- **I–V–♭VII–IV** — substitutes ♭VII for vi (C–G–B♭–F): "bluesy," adds a **chromatic descent** of
  three semitones (the ♭VII is borrowed/mixolydian). A flavor variant, not the diatonic Axis.

## 2. HARMONIC FUNCTION — why it balances bright and melancholy in one 4-bar cycle

- **I (tonic)** — stability, home, brightness.
- **V (dominant)** — tension, the upward push; wants to resolve.
- **vi (relative minor)** — the **"emotional heart."** V→vi is a **deceptive resolution** (the
  dominant denies the expected I and drops into the minor) — this is the surprise/melancholy turn,
  the single most affecting move in the cycle.
- **IV (subdominant)** — uplift / softening; vi→IV→(I) is a **plagal return** that resolves gently
  rather than with a hard authentic cadence. The cycle never slams a PAC shut, so it loops forever.

The four-bar emotional shape (from the OtoTheory analysis):
1. **I→V** (C→G): upward push, brightness.
2. **V→vi** (G→Am): the deceptive drop into melancholy — the emotional center.
3. **vi→IV→I** (Am→F→C): plagal softening back home.

Bass traces V-tension → vi minor color → tonic via the gentle subdominant. Because the loop avoids
a decisive V–I authentic cadence on the downbeat, repetition never feels closed — it just turns
over. (This is the craft-doctrine "let the harmony travel under a stable line" — foundations §3.)

## 3. THE RECOLOR MECHANIC — the load-bearing heart of this card

The reason "it doesn't get old no matter how many times you repeat it" is **common-tone
recoloring**: several scale degrees belong to THREE of the four chords, so a single sustained or
repeated melody note keeps changing its harmonic *function* while staying literally the same pitch.

**The canonical demonstration (C major, from OtoTheory — verbatim mechanic):**
> "Try sustaining the note **E** through all four chords: it's the **3rd of C (I)**, the **13th of
> G (V)**, the **5th of Am (vi)**, and the **maj7 of F (IV)**."

So one held E is, in turn:
| chord | E's function | color |
|---|---|---|
| **I** (C) | 3rd | consonant, the major-third sweetness |
| **V** (G) | 13th (an upper extension over G7/G) | shimmering tension, an unresolved color |
| **vi** (Am) | 5th | stable, the open consonance of the minor |
| **IV** (F) | **maj7** | the bright, yearning Lydian-ish glow (the most colored of the four) |

The other shared degrees behave similarly across I/vi/IV:
- **^1 (C)**: root of I → 5th of V → ♭3/minor-3rd... no — C is the ♭3 of Am? Am = A C E, so C is the
  **3rd of vi** → root of I → 5th of IV? F = F A C, C is the **5th of IV** → 4th/sus of V. So a held
  C is: root of I, the minor-3rd of vi (the melancholy color!), the 5th of IV, an added-4th over V.
- **^5 (G)**: 5th of I → **root of V** → ♭7... G in Am? Am has no G (Am7 does). G is the 5th of I,
  root of V, and the **maj7 over IV** is E not G — recheck: over IV (F A C) the bright tension note
  is E (maj7); G over F is the **9th**. So a held G = 5th of I, root of V, 9th of IV.

**The single most striking recolor is ^3 (E in C):** consonant 3rd → extension → 5th → maj7. That
arc — start sweet/grounded, become tense/floating over V, settle as the minor's 5th, then *bloom*
as the maj7 over IV — is an emotional micro-journey carried by a note that never moves. THIS is
"the melody travels because the harmony recolors beneath it," made literal. The card should make
this audible: anchor ^3 across the cycle as the recurring pivot, let the line decorate around it,
and SAVE the maj7-over-IV moment so the recolor is heard, not just stated.

Pedal-point craft (from the pedal-point sources): a repeated/held note over moving chords "starts
consonant, becomes dissonant, resolves consonant" — the constant note alternates chord-tone /
non-chord-tone status, which is exactly the recolor here. (Van Halen "Jump" = tonic-pedal cousin.)

## 4. REPERTOIRE (across idioms — proof the engine is genre-agnostic)

- **Pop/rock anthems:** "Don't Stop Believin'" (Journey, the V–vi–IV–I rotation — the delayed-tonic
  euphoria), "Let It Be" / "No Woman No Cry" family, "Someone Like You" (Adele, A major), "Girls
  Like You" (Maroon 5), "Paparazzi" (Lady Gaga, G major).
- **Country:** "Wagon Wheel" (D major, I–V–vi–IV).
- **EDM/modern minor rotation:** "Faded"/"Alone" (Alan Walker — vi–IV–I–V, the melancholy rotation).
- **Singer-songwriter:** "Cornelia Street" (Taylor Swift), the "sensitive female" vi–IV–I–V lineage.
- **Pop-punk:** "Dammit" (Blink-182, C major).
- The Wikipedia article documents **400+** songs across decades and genres.

Why so many *singable* tunes ride it: the common-tone web (§3) means a melody can sit on a small set
of anchor notes and stay consonant-or-colored over every chord — easy to sing, hard to get wrong,
and the recolor supplies the interest the static melody notes don't have to.

## 5. CRAFT TAKE-AWAYS for the card (note-level)

1. **Pick an anchor degree and recolor it.** ^3 is the strongest (3rd→13th→5th→maj7). Return to it
   on a strong beat under each chord so the ear hears the SAME note change meaning. Save its
   maj7-over-IV appearance as the bloom.
2. **The V→vi deceptive drop is the emotional event** — voice it so the melody leans into the minor
   (e.g. land ^3 as the minor's 5th, or touch ^1 as the minor's ♭3 color) rather than glossing it.
3. **The loop must not slam shut.** Avoid a hard V–I PAC mid-cycle; let the plagal vi–IV–I soften.
   A real PAC is reserved for the very end if the form wants closure.
4. **Two cycles = statement + departure** (anti-stencil, doctrine): cycle 1 plants the hook on the
   anchor; cycle 2 must DEPART — register lift, a real second idea, the apex — not a transposed copy.
5. **Breath is load-bearing.** The engine is a groove; a singable hook needs rests to be a hook, not
   wall-to-wall motion. Silence before the downbeat of a recolor makes the recolor land.
6. **Bass/strong-beat skeleton spells I–V–vi–IV.** Per the melody-craft doctrine, the line's own
   strong beats must expose the progression (downbeat = root by default; an intentional 3rd/5th on
   the beat is the *recolor mislead*, used knowingly — that IS this technique).
