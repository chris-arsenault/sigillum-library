# IMPLIED POLYPHONY / COMPOUND MELODY — making ONE line carry a full CHORD PROGRESSION (survey, NOT distilled)

The technique: **a single unaccompanied melodic line is constructed so that the listener hears not
one voice but a whole multi-part texture — a bass, an inner voice, an upper melody — and therefore
hears a complete functional CHORD PROGRESSION, with nothing else playing.** No accompaniment, no
double-stops required: the chords and their voice-leading are *encoded into the order, register, and
metric placement of the single stream of notes*, and the ear reconstructs them. This is the Baroque
craft behind the Bach solo cello and solo violin works, the A-minor flute Partita, and the Telemann
fantasias. It is the inverse of harmonizing a melody: instead of putting chords *under* a tune, you
fold the chords *into* the tune so completely that the tune alone projects them.

Pitches scientific, middle C = C4. Flats spelled "B-" = B♭ etc. This survey is BOTH the source the
card is composed from AND a durable library reference; detail is the point, not distilled into a
checklist.

---

## 1. The names (overlapping terms — they differ on emphasis, not on the phenomenon)

The phenomenon has accreted many labels; they are near-synonyms with different historical/analytic
flavor. Knowing the distinctions keeps the writing honest.

- **IMPLIED POLYPHONY** — the perceptual/analytic name: a single notated line *sounds as* two or more
  simultaneous voices. The voices are *implied* (not literally sustained) yet heard. This is the
  umbrella term used in modern theory (Open Music Theory, Wikipedia, the MTO literature).
- **COMPOUND MELODY** — the same thing named from the melody's side: one melody that is *compounded*
  out of several latent strands. Schenkerian/voice-leading writers favor this term; it foregrounds
  that the line can be *reduced* to its constituent voices.
- **POLYPHONIC MELODY / "polyphonic line"** — Open Music Theory's phrasing: "a single line that
  suggests several voice-leading patterns." Stresses that what's implied is not just chords but
  *voice-leading* (the horizontal motion of each implied part).
- **PSEUDO-POLYPHONY** — emphasizes that there is no *real* simultaneity; the polyphony is an
  illusion the composer engineers and the ear completes.
- **STYLE BRISÉ ("broken style") / style luthé** — the 17th-c. lute/harpsichord ancestor: chords
  played as broken arpeggios rather than blocked, so the harmony is spread across time. Historically
  (Niedt's *Musikalische Handleitung*, 1707; the figured-bass tradition) implied polyphony was
  described precisely as **transforming a multi-voiced harmonic progression into a single line by
  arpeggiation** — diminution of a realized continuo. The Baroque practitioner started from the
  chords and *brisé*'d them; the modern listener does the reverse and reassembles them.
- **UNFOLDING (Ausfaltung)** — the Schenkerian operation: a single line that alternates back and
  forth between two registral positions "unfolds" a two-voice interval (e.g. a 10th) into a melodic
  zig-zag. The most rigorous account of the bass-vs-upper alternation.

The WHY they're distinct from ordinary arpeggiation: a plain arpeggio (root–third–fifth, narrow,
fast, one direction) is heard as *one* chord, *one* gesture. It becomes *compound melody* only when
the line is shaped so the ear refuses to hear one stream and splits it into independent voices —
which is a matter of register, interval size, tempo, and metric placement (§3).

---

## 2. The mechanism in one sentence, then the parts

**One sentence:** widely-spaced, register-segregated notes presented in time, with the harmonic root
or implied bass parked on metrically strong beats and a separate upper line woven between, so that
*auditory stream segregation* peels the single line into a low "bass" stream and a high "melody"
stream — and the changing chords those streams spell out are heard as a progression.

The craft therefore has five working parts, each below:
1. **Arpeggiate the underlying chord** so its tones appear in the line (§3).
2. **Stream by register** — separate the implied voices by pitch distance so the ear hears them apart (§4).
3. **Anchor the implied bass** — put the chord root / lowest note on strong beats (§5).
4. **Signal harmonic rhythm** — make the moment of chord-change audible (§6).
5. **Voice-lead the implied parts** — give the bass and the upper voice their own coherent stepwise/leading motion across bars (§7).

---

## 3. ARPEGGIATION — which tones, in what order

Open Music Theory: *"Arpeggiation is the process of outlining a vertical sonority as a melodic
figure,"* ascending, descending, or leaping back and forth. The pivotal claim:

> Arpeggiation of any interval **beyond a third** will often create a *polyphonic line* — a single
> line that suggests several voice-leading patterns.

That interval-size threshold is the hinge. Adjacent thirds (a smooth do-mi-sol) read as one voice;
the moment the line *leaps a fourth or more* and then changes direction, the ear posts a seam and
starts hearing two voices. So the composer chooses chord tones *and the leaps between them* to put
seams exactly where two implied voices should part.

**Which tones, in what order (the practical recipe the Baroque used):**
- Sound the **bass/root first or lowest** in the figure (the harmonic anchor — §5).
- Spread to the **fifth and an upper chord tone** (third, or an upper octave of root/fifth) so the
  figure spans a 10th or more — wide enough to stream.
- Reserve the **top note** as the melodic-voice pitch; that is what the ear tracks as "the tune."
- Re-touch the bass each beat or each chord so the bass stream stays alive while the upper line moves.

This is *not* "play all the chord tones." It is: place each chord tone in the register of the voice
it belongs to, so the arpeggio is read as a *chord with parts*, not a scribble.

---

## 4. REGISTRAL STREAMING — the perceptual engine (why the ear hears two voices)

This is the load-bearing science. Stacy Davis's *Music Theory Online* study (MTO 17.1, "Stream
Segregation and Perceived Syncopation in the Solo Bach Works") lays out the psychoacoustics Bach was
exploiting. The findings, stated as usable thresholds:

- **A single line splits into separate perceptual streams when it has LARGE INTERVALS at a RAPID
  TEMPO.** Both conditions matter: wide leaps that are slow stay one voice; fast narrow runs stay one
  voice; *fast + wide* fractures into voices.
- **Interval threshold ≈ a perfect fourth (greater than five semitones).** Below it the notes fuse
  into one stream; above it, at speed, they segregate. The P4 is the floor.
- **V-shaped contours split more readily than linear contours.** A note that leaps up then immediately
  back down (or down-then-up) is the strongest segregation cue: the ear assigns the "out and back"
  extreme to a different voice. Linear scalar motion resists splitting (it reads as one voice walking).
- **Phenomenal accent at the seam:** *"the transition between two notes will be heard as a grouping
  boundary if its interval is larger than those that both precede and follow it."* The biggest leap in
  a local group becomes a perceived accent — and a voice-change point.

Davis's deeper point, and the reason Bach is the exemplar: *"Bach did not treat implied polyphony
solely as a melodic feature… he used implied polyphony to apply irregular accent patterns to the
isochronous surface."* The even stream of sixteenths is metrically flat; the register leaps
**create accents and voice-entries that syncopate against the beat**, so the texture has rhythmic
and contrapuntal life that the literal note-values don't show. Examples Davis analyzes: the Partita
No. 1 in B minor, the Cello Suite in G major, the G-minor Violin Sonata.

**Practical consequence for writing:** to make a second voice audible, don't merely include a low
note — *isolate it by a leap of a fourth or more and put a directional turn around it*, at a tempo
brisk enough that the ear can't track the zig-zag as one line. The register gap is the staff lines
of an invisible second staff.

## 5. ANCHORING THE IMPLIED BASS — root on the strong beat

The bass voice is heard because **the lowest note of each chord lands on the metrically strong beat
and recurs there**, establishing a register the ear reserves for "the bass." The figured-bass
heritage is explicit (The Strad, on the Bach solo works): multiple-stops and the lowest notes of
arpeggio figures *"function as harmonic anchor points… vertical snapshots in the network of the
different horizontal voices,"* and the lower notes *"suggest a functional bass line, a practice
derived from figured-bass traditions."*

Rules of thumb the repertoire follows:
- **Strong beat = implied bass.** Beat 1 (and often beat 3) carries the chord's bass tone. The ear
  uses metric strength to decide which note is "the bass note of this chord."
- **Lowest note = root (or the intended bass inversion).** Put the root lowest for root-position
  harmony; deliberately put the third or fifth lowest to imply a first/second-inversion chord —
  inversion is controllable even with one line.
- **Re-articulate or pedal the bass to keep it alive.** If the bass should hold across a bar (a pedal
  point), the line keeps *returning* to that low pitch on strong beats so the ear sustains it
  mentally even while the upper voice moves (§ the BWV 1007 opening, where low G is re-touched and
  read as a held tonic pedal).

---

## 6. SIGNALLING HARMONIC RHYTHM — where one chord ends and the next begins

A single line must *announce* its chord changes, because there is no accompaniment to change under
it. The devices:

- **Change the bass note on the downbeat.** The clearest signal: a new low note on beat 1 = new
  chord. Harmonic rhythm is read off the rate of strong-beat bass change (Mertens on BWV 1007: *"the
  harmonic rhythm typically changes at the bar lines,"* one chord per bar in the opening, with the
  first 8 or 16 notes of the bar presenting the new harmony).
- **Introduce the diagnostic chord tone.** A chord change is confirmed when the note that *defines*
  the new chord (its third, its seventh, a chromatic alteration) enters — e.g. the C# that signals
  V/V or a tonicized dominant; the B- that turns the harmony toward D minor. These "tell" notes mark
  the chord boundary even when the bass is shared.
- **Cadential leading-tone resolution.** At cadences the implied voices must perform the real
  voice-leading: Open Music Theory stresses that the polyphonic melody must express *an actual
  voice-leading pattern* — *"the leading tone should resolve."* The 7→1 and 4→3 motions, distributed
  across the line's registers, are how a cadence is heard as a cadence in one voice.
- **Transition figures before a change.** Mertens marks "transition" gestures on the last beat before
  a modulation (BWV 1007 bars 4, 5, 7, 12) — a scalar run or pivot that ushers in the new harmony,
  so the seam is prepared rather than abrupt.

## 7. VOICE-LEADING THE IMPLIED PARTS — each strand moves coherently across the bar

The illusion only holds if each *implied* voice obeys real counterpoint over time — otherwise the
ear stops believing in the separate voices. Two strands to lead:

- **The implied BASS line** moves slowly and stepwise/by structural leap, like a real continuo bass.
  In BWV 1007 the bass is a tonic G pedal for the opening, then *descends stepwise* (by implication)
  through the following bars toward a structural F# / dominant — a coherent bass *line*, not random
  low notes.
- **The implied UPPER line** carries the melodic interest and resolves dissonances. In the Chaconne
  (BWV 1004) the upper voice traces an arch (an ascending P4 contour) separated by compound intervals
  from an implied bass that descends by a two-note semitone pattern — two independent contours from
  one line. Suspensions live here too (the 7–6 suspensions over the A pedal in BWV 1007 mm. 29–31:
  the dissonant 7th and its resolution to the 6th are both single notes in the line, but heard as a
  held upper voice resolving against the held bass).
- **Inner voice as connective tissue.** A middle-register pitch held or re-touched (e.g. the
  repeated D in the BWV 1007 opening figure) supplies the chord's fifth and binds bass to top — a
  third implied voice, the "filler," which is why these works read as *three*-part, not two.

The Strad summarizes the test: scholars can *"reduce most of the movements… to convincing
three-part pieces while still respecting the rules of counterpoint."* If the reduction is good
counterpoint, the implied polyphony was real.

## 8. THE CANONICAL EXEMPLAR — BWV 1007 Prelude, bar by bar (note level)

The G-major Cello Suite No. 1 Prelude is the textbook case: an unbroken stream of sixteenths that
the ear hears as a chord progression with a bass.

**Bar 1 — Tonic, G major (I), as compound melody.** The bar is two identical halves of six
sixteenths. The figure (scientific pitch):

```
beat 1:  G2   D3   B3   A3   B3   D3        (then the same six notes repeat for beat 3)
         └bass┘ └────upper / inner voices───┘
```
- **G2** on the strong beat = the implied **bass** (the chord root, lowest note). It is re-touched at
  the head of each half-bar, so the ear reads a **held tonic pedal G**.
- **D3** = the **inner voice** (the fifth), the pivot the line keeps returning to — binds bass to top.
- **B3–A3–B3** = the **upper voice** (the melody): third of the chord with a neighbor-tone A3
  decorating it. The leap up from G2/D3 to B3 (a 10th / a sixth) is far past the P4 streaming
  threshold, so the ear cleanly splits "low G–D" from "high B–A–B." Three implied voices from six
  notes.

**Bars 1–4 — the opening progression made audible.** One chord per bar, harmonic rhythm at the
bar line:
- **m.1: I** (G major) over the G pedal.
- **m.2: IV** (C major / or ii depending on edition reading) — the figure's *lowest* and strong-beat
  notes shift to spell the subdominant; new bass note on the downbeat = new chord.
- **m.3: V7** (D7, dominant) — the chord-defining C-natural (the 7th) and F# (the leading tone) enter
  the figure; the ear hears the dominant by its diagnostic tones.
- **m.4: I** (G major) — resolution; the leading tone F# → G and the 7th C → B are performed inside
  the single line. A complete **I–IV–V7–I** authentic-cadence frame, in one unaccompanied voice.

**The large-scale progression (Part 1, mm. 1–22).** Tonic G pedal sustained through ~m.6; the
implied bass then *walks down stepwise* (by implication) toward F# (≈ m.13, functioning as V/vi),
returns toward the tonic region, then a chain of **secondary dominants** drives to the structural
**dominant A7 in third inversion at m.21**, prolonged through the fermata (m.22). **Part 2
(mm. 22–41)** is essentially one long **dominant prolongation**: a double pedal (low **D** and **A**
a fifth above) in mm. 31–37 with 7–6 suspensions over the A, chromatic ascent in the upper implied
voice, finally **resolving to tonic G at m.42**. The whole prelude is thus a single huge tonic→
dominant→tonic arch — *audible from one line* because the implied bass and upper voices are led
coherently the entire way. (Mertens; John Hall analysis.)

## 9. THE WIDER REPERTOIRE (each shows a different facet)

- **Chaconne, BWV 1004 (solo violin).** Upper voice = ascending-P4 arch; implied bass = two-note
  descending semitone (the chaconne's lament tetrachord), separated by *compound* intervals — the
  clearest case of two contours from one line. Also uses real multiple-stops as periodic "vertical
  snapshots" that confirm the implied harmony.
- **Presto, G-minor Sonata BWV 1001 / Fuga BWV 1001 (solo violin).** The Fuga literally *implies a
  three-voice fugue* — subject entries in different registers heard as separate contrapuntal voices
  in a mostly single line. The Presto shows fast V-contour streaming.
- **Allegro, Violin Sonata No. 2 (BWV 1003).** *Imitative dialogue between the two lower implied
  voices* — implied polyphony rich enough to carry imitation, not just chords.
- **Partita in A minor for solo flute, BWV 1013.** The hardest case — the flute cannot play
  multiple stops at all, so *everything* is implied by register and figuration; the Allemande is wall
  to wall compound melody, proving the technique needs no chordal capacity, only the line.
- **Telemann, 12 Fantasias for solo violin / for solo flute (TWV 40).** The galant sibling of the
  Bach works: implied polyphony with lighter textures, frequent register-toggling to imply a
  bass-and-tune two-part texture, and clear strong-beat bass anchoring.
- **Vivaldi, Violin Concerto RV 317.** Open Music Theory's worked example: the solo violin plays a
  *polyphonic-melody diminution* of the accompanying strings' chords — implied polyphony as
  ornamented realization of a known progression (the Baroque's own "start from the chords" route).

## 10. THE PROCESS (how to build a progression-audible single line)

A constructive recipe, distilled from the above (this is the *method*, the survey above is the *why*):

1. **Commit the progression and a bass line first.** Decide the chords per bar AND the bass note
   (with inversion) for each — you are writing a continuo part you will then *dissolve* into the line.
2. **Sketch the two outer implied voices as real counterpoint.** A slow stepwise bass; an upper
   melody that resolves its dissonances and leading tones. Add an inner voice (often the fifth) if you
   want three-part richness. Verify it as good 2- or 3-part counterpoint *before* arpeggiating.
3. **Assign each voice a register band.** Bass low, melody high, with at least a P4 (preferably a
   6th–10th) gap between bass and the next voice so streaming will fire.
4. **Arpeggiate into one line, beat by beat.** Put the bass note lowest and on the strong beat;
   weave the inner/upper voices above it; use *leaps beyond a third with a directional turn* at every
   point two voices should separate. Keep the tempo brisk enough to stream.
5. **Mark each chord change on the downbeat.** New strong-beat bass note + the new chord's diagnostic
   tone (its 3rd/7th/chromatic alteration) at the bar line, so harmonic rhythm is audible.
6. **Re-touch held voices.** For a pedal or a sustained upper note, *return to that pitch* on strong
   beats so the ear keeps it sounding while other voices move.
7. **Lead the bass and top coherently across the whole span.** The bass should be a *line* (pedal,
   then stepwise descent, then structural leaps to dominant/tonic); the top a *shaped melody*. Test by
   reducing your line back to 2–3 staves — if the reduction is convincing counterpoint, the implied
   polyphony is real.
