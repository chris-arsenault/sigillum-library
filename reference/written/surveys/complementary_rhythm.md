# Answer-in-the-Gap: Complementary Rhythm, Hocket & Fills (survey, NOT distilled)

The technique: **when the melody rests, another voice sounds in that gap** — most powerful when the
melody is silent on a STRONG beat (the accompaniment hits the felt-but-empty downbeat), or slightly
OFFSET for a stutter/limp. The voices interlock so the composite surface keeps moving though each
voice has holes. **This is an ENSEMBLE device, NOT piano-only — strongest when the lead is a
DIFFERENT COLOUR from the accompaniment** (a clarinet/woodwind melody over a piano comp; §4g).
Card: **D9_REINFORCE_CONTRAST** (clarinet + piano comp). Related existing card: D1 GAP_FILL_PAIR.
Pitches scientific, middle C = C4.

## 1. The names (overlapping but DISTINCT — they differ on what unit is traded)

- **HOCKET** — note-level interlock, the strict case. Old French *hoquet* = "hiccup"; codified by
  Franco of Cologne (*Ars cantus mensurabilis*, c.1280) as *truncatio* of sound by rests coordinated
  ACROSS voices. One intended line is segmented in time: voice A rests exactly where B sounds; neither
  is a complete melody alone — their union (the "**resultant**" line) reconstitutes one gapless tune.
  That mutual-exclusivity is the litmus test. Perceptual: close, rapid interlocking attacks **fuse into
  one stream** ("a melody bouncing around the ensemble"); tempo-dependent (slow = audible stutter,
  fast = a seamless line no single player could play). Repertoire: the *In seculum* hockets (Bamberg/
  Montpellier codices), Machaut *Hoquetus David*; Stravinsky (*Les Noces*, *Symphonies of Wind
  Instruments*), minimalism (Andriessen *Hoketus*, Reich), Balinese *kotekan* (interlocking polos+
  sangsih: *nyog cag*/*telu*/*empat*), Ba-Benzélé/Mbuti forest polyphony.
- **COMPLEMENTARY RHYTHM / "fill the gaps / fill the long note"** — the orchestration umbrella, the
  best name for the general idea. A countermelody "fills in with short notes when the melody is sitting
  on long ones, and gets out of the way with longer notes when the melody moves." Supporting terms:
  **composite rhythm** (collapse all voices to one staff, mark an attack wherever ANY voice attacks);
  **surface rhythm** (foreground note-motion) vs **harmonic rhythm**; **attack-point rhythm** (Yeston,
  *The Stratification of Musical Rhythm*, 1976 — "the absolute surface of articulated movement", the
  precise term for "the union of attacks"). Rimsky-Korsakov's "melody in relief" achieved temporally.
- **CALL-AND-RESPONSE / ANTIPHONY / RESPONSORIAL** — phrase-level: two COMPLETE phrases in dialogue
  (responsorial = leader vs chorus; antiphonal = two equal groups; jazz "trading fours"). Differs from
  hocket: trades whole sentences, each self-standing, vs fracturing one sentence note-by-note.
- **FILL** — subordinate, asymmetric answer (jazz/pop/gospel/drumming): "play in the holes", answer
  the singer in the breaths; match fill length to the gap (half-beat gap = quick decisive fill,
  full-beat = longer phrase). Subordinate, unlike a co-equal call-and-response or an attention-grabbing
  solo/break.
- **DOVETAILING / INTERLOCKING** — hand a continuous line between voices so entrances OVERLAP at the
  seam (a tied/doubled note on the downbeat); hocket is its limiting extreme.
- **KLANGFARBENMELODIE / POINTILLISM** — a line distributed across timbres, often with rests
  (Schoenberg coined it, *Harmonielehre* 1911; strict sense = a held sonority recolored, e.g. Op.16
  No.3 "Farben", chord C4–G#4–B4–E5–A5; Webern's pointillism = the line shattered into isolated notes
  across instruments/registers, e.g. his orchestration of Bach's *Ricercar a 6* — yet "the theme must
  not appear disintegrated").
- **DURCHBROCHENE ARBEIT** ("openwork/pierced") — the Viennese-Classical ancestor: one line parceled
  in fragments among instruments, continuity-preserving (Haydn quartets, *Eroica* I, Brahms).
- **STOP-TIME / PUNCTUATION** — the band stops grooving and stabs isolated beats while the soloist
  fills the silence (Joplin *Stoptime Rag*, Armstrong *Potato Head Blues*).

**Which name fits "accompaniment hits the melody's strong-beat rest"?** The note-level answer-in-the-
gap = **complementary rhythm** ("fill the gap"); if it reconstitutes one line split between hands so
neither is complete = **hocket**; a subordinate answer to a featured tune = **fill**. The expressive
engine (the answer lands on a felt-but-silent strong beat) is metric theory, §2.

## 2. The strong-beat-rest application (why a SILENT strong beat still carries accent)

GTTM (Lerdahl & Jackendoff 1983): metrical accent is a position in an abstract beat-grid, NOT an
acoustic event — "**a beat need not be associated with the onset of a note**"; the grid stays
isochronous cognitively. Cooper & Meyer (1960): "**a sense of regular pulses, once established, tends
to be continued in the mind and musculature of the listener, even though the sound has stopped.**"
Dynamic Attending (Large & Jones): attention is oscillatory and **peaks anticipatorily at strong
positions** (ERP shows larger on-beat than off-beat responses, even before onset). So when the melody
RESTS on a strong beat, the listener still projects that beat as strong; an attack placed there by
another voice (1) reads as **accent-by-surprise** (the foreground implied silence), (2) **anchors the
meter** the floating melody is suspended over, (3) satisfies a peaking expectation. Repertoire: Beethoven
5 opens with an 8th REST on beat 1; reggae "one drop" strikes kick+snare together on beat 3.

Example (4/4, *to spec*): melody `E5(q) G5(q) [REST q] D5(q)` rests on beat 3; accompaniment strikes
**C2 + G4** exactly on the silent beat 3 — the accent lands in the hole.

## 3. The offset / stutter variant (displacement)

Krebs (*Fantasy Pieces*, 1999): one offset note = a syncopation; a run of identically-offset notes =
**displacement dissonance** (a persistent layer out of phase, "displacement index" e.g. D 3+1).
Direction/amount of offset changes the feel:
- **Push / anticipation (early):** note just before the beat — energetic, forward drive (the next
  chord's tone arrives early).
- **Lay-back / drag (late):** note at the tail of the beat — relaxed, heavy; a suspension/tie is the
  late flavor (old note lingers over the strong beat, denying it a fresh onset).
- **Limp/stutter:** the answer sits offset against another part holding the center → a dragged echo.
- **Amount:** an **8th** offset is heard as a distinct answer/echo (nameable limp or push); a **16th**
  offset is felt as grease/feel, not a separate event.

Example (stutter, inner voice an 8th LATE): melody `E5` on beat 1 (held), inner `E4` on "1&"; `C5` on
beat 3, inner `C4` on "3&". The trailing answer limps.

## 4. Scenario-specific applications

- **(a) Gentle ballad fill** in the melody's breath: melody rests on beat 3 → a rootless stab or a
  short RH run answers, length matched to the gap.
- **(b) Driving/funk interlock:** bass + comp + melody each sparse and full of rests, together tiling
  a continuous 16th surface (James Brown's interlocking guitars — "the rests create the space"); the
  groove anchored "on the One."
- **(c) Orchestral "fill the long note"** at a phrase-end: melody holds/rests → a countermelody/harp/
  wind run fills the bar and resolves or anticipates into the next phrase (composite stays continuous).
- **(d) Hocket as the theme:** the tune IS the interlock (Stravinsky/Andriessen/gamelan); a rising
  scale split note-by-note between two voices at the 8th pulse.
- **(e) Call-and-response between sections:** a complete phrase answered by a complete phrase in a
  contrasting register (big-band brass-vs-sax).
- **(f) Punctuation in silence:** everything rests, then a single chord stabs the next strong beat
  (Haydn "Surprise" m.16) — the silence is the setup.
- **(g) WIND LEAD + PIANO COMP, comp alternating REINFORCE/CONTRAST (the strongest version).** The
  lead is a different colour (clarinet/oboe/flute) so the two layers never fuse; the wind's breaths
  are built-in gaps. The piano comp converses by ALTERNATING two stances toward the lead's PLAYED
  notes: **reinforce** (hit/double WITH the lead — rhythmic + harmonic agreement, a shared accent) and
  **contrast** (answer in the lead's rests, or move against a held note with a surprising harmony /
  contrary motion). The alternation is the life of it — pure reinforcement is doubling (dead), pure
  contrast loses the lock. This is card **D9_REINFORCE_CONTRAST**. The timbral contrast does what
  dynamics/register do in solo piano: keeps the melody in relief while the comp stays active.

## 5. Mechanics (how to construct it)

1. Write the melody first; **map its onsets and rests** on a beat-grid, marking strong-beat rests and
   long-note gaps.
2. Put an answer in each gap: a **bass note** (anchors meter — strongest on a strong-beat rest), an
   **inner-voice fragment** (passing/neighbor), a **chord stab** (rootless, short), or a **fill run**
   (length matched to the gap).
3. **Complementary value-classes:** the answer is active (short values) only while the melody is inert
   (long/rest), and recedes when the melody is busy — no two competing fast streams.
4. **Voice-leading payoff:** the gap-filler should **resolve** the held harmony, **anticipate** the
   next melodic arrival, or make a passing/neighbor connection — double duty (surface + counterpoint).
5. **Continuous composite:** collapse all voices mentally — wherever the melody has a hole, some voice
   attacks, so the union of attacks is a continuous stream (momentum, no dead air).
6. **Tune the offset:** dead-on the gap = clarity/anchor; 8th early = push; 8th late = limp/lay-back;
   16th off = grease. A consistent offset → Krebsian displacement (a persistent out-of-phase layer).
