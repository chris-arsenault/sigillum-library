# 16TH NOTES AS A MOVING MELODIC LINE & RUNNING COUNTERPOINT (survey, NOT distilled)

The technique: **a voice in continuous fast notes (16ths) that is a genuine MELODY — it goes
somewhere (contour, an apex reached once, gap-fill / leap-recovery, sequence, a destination
cadence) — NOT a texture effect.** And its richest form, **RUNNING COUNTERPOINT: two (or more)
such lines moving in 16ths AT ONCE, each independent yet consonant** — the *Art of the Fugue* /
two-part-Invention / toccata quality. 16ths here are normal, common content in complex music: a
fast melody, running inner voices, a fugue subject in diminution, toccata passagework. This is the
OPPOSITE of the library's current 16th content, which is ~3:1 oscillation/repeat over real moving
lines and has ZERO running counterpoint. Pitches scientific, middle C = C4; flats "B-" = B♭.

This survey is BOTH the source the card is composed from AND a durable library reference. Detail is
the point; it is deliberately not distilled into a checklist.

## 0. THE NAMES (distinguished — these are NOT the same thing, and three of them are NOT this card)

The cluster of overlapping terms, separated on their actual mechanics:

- **MOTO PERPETUO / PERPETUUM MOBILE** ("perpetual motion") — Grove/Wikipedia: "a rapidly executed
  and persistently maintained figuration, usually of notes of equal length," an entire piece or
  passage of an *unbroken stream of equal notes at a rapid tempo*. The notes are 16ths (or 8ths at a
  blazing tempo) — **measured, named pitches**. Paganini *Moto perpetuo* Op. 11, Mendelssohn,
  Weber, Ravel "Toccata," Rimsky "Flight of the Bumblebee." The defining demand of the idiom (BMInt,
  classical-music.com): the relentless stream must stay *melodic* — "surprisingly melodic, which has
  allowed the piece to maintain its charm" — or it collapses into an etude. THIS is the single-line
  card's genre.
- **TOCCATA passagework** — "touch-piece"; brilliant keyboard/organ running figuration, often
  16ths, showing off touch and momentum, frequently over (or alternating with) sustained or chordal
  anchors. Bach Toccatas (BWV 910–916, the D-minor BWV 565 opening flourish), Schumann *Toccata*
  Op. 7, Prokofiev, Ravel. Differs from pure moto perpetuo by its *rhetorical* breaks — a run lands
  on a chord, a pedal point holds, then it runs again.
- **RUNNING COUNTERPOINT / two-part invention / fugal stretto in 16ths** — the polyphonic case:
  **two or more independent voices each moving melodically, the 16ths shared or handed between
  them**, governed by species/invertible-counterpoint rules. Bach two-part *Inventions* (BWV
  772–786), the *Art of the Fugue* (BWV 1080), the WTC fugues in stretto. THIS is the 2-voice card's
  genre. The hallmark is **rhythmic complementarity**: when one voice runs 16ths the other holds or
  moves in 8ths/quarters, and they swap — the stream is *continuous in the union* but *distributed*,
  so two real melodies are audible at once.
- **COMPOUND MELODY / implied polyphony** — a SINGLE running line whose leaps make the ear split it
  into two (or more) registral strands: a high "voice" and a low "voice" alternating within one
  16th stream (Bach solo-violin Preludes/Partitas, the E-major Partita Prelude = "uninterrupted
  16ths" that the ear hears as two voices). This is *how a single moto-perpetuo line gets polyphonic
  depth without a second player* — load-bearing for the single-line card. (The library's F2 already
  does this in 8ths; the 16th version is new.)

**NOT this card (the distractors — the brief is explicit):**
- **TREMOLO** — unmeasured or 32nds, a *shimmer/effect*, NO melodic content (two notes or a chord
  shaken). The library's P11 owns this. A tremolo is NOT a 16th melodic line.
- **OSCILLATION / NEIGHBOR ROCKING** (F4 NEIGHBOR_OSCILLATION) — rocking two adjacent notes
  (A3↔B♭3), "motion circles, never spells." This is the *anti*-technique: 2 distinct pitches, no
  destination. The audit found the library is 3:1 this over real lines.
- **REPEATED-CELL / TOCCATA-ALTERNATION** (F6) — one pitch traded between desks. Again ~1–2 distinct
  pitches; rhythm-as-texture, not a moving line.
- **BROKEN-CHORD ARPEGGIO** — spelling a chord up and down in 16ths (the C-major Prelude figure). A
  16th stream, but it *spells harmony* rather than *singing a line*; the library rations this to
  ≤1/3 of figuration. A moving line is the type-3/4 alternative (compound melody / scalar current),
  NOT the arpeggio.

**Which fits THIS card slot?** Two cards: **(a)** a single **moto-perpetuo / toccata running line**
that is genuinely melodic (with compound-melody depth), keyboard; **(b)** a **two-part running
counterpoint** (invention/fugue-like), 2 voices in 16ths with complementarity and invertible
counterpoint. Together they fill the exact hole: real moving 16th lines + simultaneous independent
moving lines.

## 1. THE MECHANICS & THE WHY — how a fast line MOVES instead of running like an etude

The melody craft (`melody_theory_foundations.md`) is the floor; the 16th idiom adds its own devices.
A running line is *still a line* — it must pass the wandering diagnostic — but it has special
problems (it can't pause, it's too fast to hear every note as structural) and special tools.

### 1a. The deep line under the surface (Schenker, §0 of melody craft) — the anti-etude key
The single most important fact: **a great moto-perpetuo line is a SLOW melody elaborated by fast
notes.** Under Bach's E-major Partita Prelude or his Invention 16ths there is a step-wise structural
descent (an Urlinie) moving at roughly one structural pitch per beat or half-bar; the 16ths *fill
between* those structural pitches with passing tones, neighbors, and arpeggiations. The etude
failure is fast notes with NO deep line — a scale or a rocked pair that goes nowhere. **Build the
slow line first (the chord tones on the strong 16ths of each beat), THEN connect them with
passing/neighbor motion.** This is why the "distinct-pitch count" test works: a real line touches
many pitches *because it is travelling between structural tones*, not circling.

### 1b. Chord-tone framework + connectors (the moto-perpetuo grammar)
The verified technique (The Strad on Bach's solo works; the compound-melody sources): the strong
positions of each beat (the 1st and often the 3rd 16th) carry **chord tones**; the weak 16ths are
**passing tones** (step between two chord tones, filling a 3rd) and **neighbor tones** (step away
and back). So one beat of C-major might be `C–D–E–F` (C, E chord tones; D, F passing/escape) or
`E–D–C–B` (E, C chord tones; D passing, B neighbor of C). The line is *harmonically legible* on the
beat and *melodically smooth* between beats. This is the type-4 SCALAR_CURRENT idea (F3) extended
into a sustained melody.

### 1c. Gap-fill / leap-recovery in a fast line (the melodic craft, §1, applied)
A run that is *only* stepwise = a scale = etude. The cure is the periodic **leap** (a 3rd, 4th, 5th,
or octave) that opens a gap, **recovered by step in the opposite direction** — exactly the cantus-
firmus rule, but at 16th speed it also doubles as the compound-melody device (1d). Mendelssohn's and
Bach's running lines are *mostly* stepwise with a leap every beat or two; the leaps give the line
*shape and joints* so it is not a featureless scale. ≤2 leaps in a row; consecutive same-direction
leaps only if they outline a triad (an arpeggiated 16th gesture).

### 1d. COMPOUND MELODY / implied polyphony (the depth trick — verified)
"Compound melody… a single melodic line on paper sounds as two or more simultaneous voices… realized
by alternating back and forth between notes of different implied melodies… all the more effective
when using bigger intervals" (The Strad). In 16ths: alternate a HIGH strand and a LOW strand within
one beat — e.g. `G5–C5–A5–C5–B5–C5` lets C5 register as a pedal "lower voice" while G5–A5–B5 ascends
as an "upper voice." The ear splits the single fast line into two. The shape "implies accents in a
hidden rhythm" — the registral peaks become the heard melody, the held inner pitch the accompaniment.
**This is how one running line teaches polyphony without a second player** — the load-bearing device
for card (a).

### 1e. SEQUENCE — the engine of travel in a running line (verified, melody craft §3)
The most idiomatic way a 16th line *gets somewhere*: state a 1-beat (or 1-bar) melodic cell, then
**transpose it down a step / down a 3rd / around the circle of fifths**. Bach Invention 1: "fragments
of the theme are often sequenced"; the augmented-bass-vs-inverted-treble pair "creates a sequence
that will modulate to G major." A descending-5ths sequence (cell on I, then on IV, then vii°, iii…)
gives the line a *direction* and a *destination* while the surface stays continuous 16ths. The
sequence is what distinguishes a *composed* run from an improvised noodle.

### 1f. The single apex, once (melody craft §6) — even at speed
A running line still obeys the arch: one high point, reached once (ideally leapt to), on a strong
position, NOT at the very end; range ≤ a 10th–12th. A moto perpetuo that climbs to its top note
three times has no climax. Plan the registral arc across the whole card: low → apex near the
3/4 point → descent to the cadence. (Paganini/Mendelssohn moto perpetui all arch.)

### 1g. The DESTINATION cadence — a run must LAND
The line is continuous but it is *going to a cadence*. The final bar slows the structural rhythm
(approach `^2–^1` or `^7–^1` by step under the 16ths, or break the stream to a chord) so the ear
hears arrival, not a tape cut. Toccatas land their runs on a chord; an invention drives the 16ths
into a cadential trill or a clean PAC. **A run that just stops is an etude; a run that cadences is a
piece.**

## 2. RUNNING COUNTERPOINT — how TWO 16th lines stay independent yet consonant

The polyphonic case (card b). Sources: Open Music Theory / Gettysburg "Double Counterpoint at the
Octave"; teoria.com invertible counterpoint; the Bach Invention analyses (schoolofcomposition,
teoria BWV 772, musictheory.pugetsound "Invention Expositions").

### 2a. RHYTHMIC COMPLEMENTARITY — the core of two-voice independence
The two voices must NOT both run 16ths in lock-step (that fuses them into parallel motion = one
thick voice). The invention idiom: **one voice runs 16ths while the other moves in 8ths / holds /
rests, and they SWAP** — so the *union* surface is continuous 16ths but each voice has its own
rhythmic profile and the ear tracks two melodies. Bach Invention 1: the subject (16ths) in the
treble against the countersubject (`C–B–C–D`, slower) in the bass, then inverted. The complementary
"answer in the gap" is the same engine the library's D1/D9 already teach — here applied so the
16ths are *handed between* the two contrapuntal lines. (At full stretto BOTH can run 16ths briefly,
but the steady state is alternation.)

### 2b. IMITATION at a delay — the invention opening
The standard exposition (musictheory.pugetsound "Invention Expositions"): voice 1 states the subject
(a 1-bar 16th cell); voice 2 enters with the SAME subject an octave lower (or a 5th up = the answer)
**one bar later**, while voice 1 continues with the countersubject. The delayed entry is what makes
two independent lines from one idea. Bach Invention 1 subject: **C–D–E–F–D–E–C** then leaps to **G**
(16ths), imitated an octave below in bar 2 a 5th higher (G–A–B–C…). Card (b) uses a real imitative
entry.

### 2c. INVERTIBLE (double) COUNTERPOINT at the octave — the independence guarantee
"Two lines that work as counterpoint regardless of the ordering of the voices" (teoria). Inverted at
the octave, intervals become their complements: **unison↔octave, 3rd↔6th, 4th↔5th, 2nd↔7th**. The
*constraint that produces independence*: the **perfect 5th inverts to a perfect 4th, which is a
dissonance** — so a P5 on a strong beat between the voices can't be treated as a stable consonance if
the passage will invert. Bach Invention 6: mm. 1–4 return as mm. 5–8 with the voices swapped (lower
goes on top). **Composing the two lines so they're consonant in 3rds/6ths/10ths on the beats — and
avoiding parallel 5ths/8ves between them — is exactly what keeps them independent and lets them
trade register.** The card writes a passage and then *re-presents it inverted* (treble line to the
bass, bass to the treble) to teach this.

### 2d. CONTRARY motion + 3rds/6ths — keeping two fast lines from colliding
Two 16th lines stay legible when they predominantly move in **contrary or oblique motion** (one up
while the other down) and meet on **imperfect consonances (3rds, 6ths, 10ths)** on the strong 16ths
of each beat. Parallel 3rds/6ths for a beat are a sweetener (the library's F5 DOUBLE_THIRDS), but a
whole passage of parallel 3rds = one voice in disguise. The independence comes from *different
contours*: where line A leaps up, line B steps down. This is the OMT first-species rule (no parallel
perfect consonances; favor contrary motion) running underneath the 16th surface.

### 2e. The episode / sequence — where two running lines TRAVEL together
Between subject entries, an invention spins an **episode**: a short motive (often the subject's tail)
**sequenced** in BOTH voices simultaneously, descending the circle of fifths, modulating. This is
where the two 16th lines are most overtly "running counterpoint" — both active, both sequencing, in
invertible counterpoint, driving to the next key. Card (b)'s middle is an episode: both voices in
16ths, contrary-motion sequence down a 5th.

## 3. EXACT-PITCH EXEMPLARS (the note-level models)

### 3a. Bach, Invention No. 1 in C major, BWV 772 (the two-voice model)
- **Subject (treble, m.1, 16ths):** `C4 D4 E4 F4 D4 E4 C4` then (8th) leap up to `G4`. Eight notes,
  three 4-note motifs: (a) scalar `C-D-E-F`; (b) `D-E-C-?` turn; (c) the swing C↔B neighbor.
  **Distinct pitches in the subject: C, D, E, F, G — 5 distinct, genuinely moving + one leap.**
- **Imitation:** bass answers the subject an octave lower in m.1 (tail clipped), then m.2 = m.1 a
  5th higher (the line moves to G). Voices trade subject/countersubject.
- **Countersubject:** the slower `C–B–C–D` against the running subject — *rhythmic complementarity*
  in action (one runs, one walks).
- **Development:** subject by **inversion** (ascending intervals become descending), by
  **augmentation** in the bass (16ths→8ths), **sequenced** to modulate to G. Episodes spin the
  motif in both voices.
- WHY it is the model: minimal material (one 1-bar cell), maximal independence, every contrapuntal
  device (imitation, inversion, augmentation, sequence, invertible counterpoint), and the 16ths
  always *spell a moving line*, never rock.

### 3b. Bach, Invention No. 6 in E major (invertible counterpoint model)
mm. 1–4 reappear as mm. 5–8 with the two voices **swapped in register** (double counterpoint at the
octave / sometimes analyzed at the 12th). The exact device card (b) imitates: write a 16th passage,
then re-present it with treble and bass exchanged.

### 3c. Bach, Prelude in C major, WTC I, BWV 846 (what NOT to do for a MELODIC line)
Continuous 16ths, but they **spell broken chords** (Cmaj → ii⁷ → V⁷…). Five-voice harmony, a slow
bass+tenor under arpeggiation. This is the *arpeggio* case the library rations — included here as the
boundary: a 16th stream that is harmony-spelling, NOT a moving melody. Card (a) must do the opposite —
a *line*, not a chord broken up.

### 3d. Bach, Partita No. 3 in E major, Prelude, BWV 1006 (compound melody in pure 16ths)
"Uninterrupted 16ths" on a *monophonic* violin that the ear hears as **two or three voices** by
register-alternation — the compound-melody model for card (a)'s single line. A high pedal E
alternates with a rising/falling lower strand, etc.

### 3e. Paganini, Moto perpetuo Op. 11 / Mendelssohn / Rimsky "Flight of the Bumblebee"
Pure moto-perpetuo: relentless 16ths that *arch* (chromatic and diatonic runs with a clear registral
climb-and-fall), stay melodic via chord-tone framework + chromatic passing tones, and *land* on a
cadence. Models for the single-line card's continuity, arch, and "surprisingly melodic" demand.

### 3f. Beyond classical
- **Jazz:** bebop "running 8ths/16ths" lines (Charlie Parker) are exactly chord-tone framework +
  chromatic passing/enclosure connectors + a target note on the strong beat — the same moto-perpetuo
  grammar in swing. Bach's chorale-prelude running lines are the ancestor.
- **Film/game:** "perpetual motion" string/woodwind ostinato *that is a line* (not a tremolo) —
  e.g. driving running-16th violin figures in action scoring that outline a melody, not just shimmer.
- **Folk/fiddle:** reels and hornpipes are running-8th/16th moto-perpetuo melodies with built-in
  gap-fill leaps and a clear tune — proof the idiom is a *melody*, not an effect.

## 4. WHY THE LIBRARY'S CURRENT 16th CARDS FAIL (so this card doesn't repeat it)
Measured from the cards:
- **F4 NEIGHBOR_OSCILLATION:** the Vn2 line rocks `A3↔B♭3` for 12 beats — **2 distinct pitches**.
  "Motion circles, never spells." By design an oscillation; but the library leans on this as its 16th
  content.
- **F6 TOCCATA_ALTERNATION:** one pitch traded between desks — **~1–2 distinct pitches**, rhythm-as-
  texture.
- **P8 CASCADE_ALTERNATE / K6 CADENZA_BURST:** 16th *runs*, but scalar waterfalls / arpeggio sweeps —
  gestural figuration, not a sustained *melody* with contour/apex/cadence; and single-voice.
- **F2 COMPOUND_MELODY:** genuinely moving and polyphonic — but in **8ths**, not a 16th running line.
- **ZERO running counterpoint:** no card has 2+ independent voices each moving melodically in 16ths
  at once. The whole invention/fugue/toccata-duet idiom is absent.

**The fix, stated as the card's test:** every 16th run must touch **many distinct pitches** (a moving
line, ~8–14 distinct per bar, not 2–3 rocking), trace a **contour with one apex and a destination
cadence**, and — in card (b) — there must be **2 simultaneous independent moving lines** verifiable
by counting both voices' distinct pitches and their rhythmic complementarity.

## 5. WHAT THIS TELLS THE CARDS

- **Card (a) RUNNING_LINE_MOTO (keyboard, piano):** a single moto-perpetuo / toccata 16th line that
  is a real melody — chord-tone framework on the beats, passing/neighbor connectors, periodic
  recovered leaps that double as **compound-melody** register-alternation (a heard upper voice over an
  implied pedal), **sequenced** to travel, one **apex**, a **destination cadence**. Under it a sparse
  LH anchor (a true bass *line* / pedal, not a chord pump) so the card is about the running line. ≈6
  bars. Re-scorable to a solo violin (the Bach Partita home) or a wind. C major (the moto-perpetuo /
  invention home key) so it re-fits any major key by transposition.
- **Card (b) RUNNING_COUNTERPOINT (two voices — piano RH/LH, or two strings/winds):** an
  invention/fugue duet — **imitative entry at a 1-bar delay**, **rhythmic complementarity** (the
  16ths handed between the voices; when one runs, the other walks), an **episode** with **both**
  voices in 16ths in **contrary-motion sequence** down the 5ths, **invertible counterpoint** (a
  passage re-presented with the voices swapped), consonant on 3rds/6ths/10ths, no parallel
  perfect consonances, driving to a PAC. ≈8 bars. C major / a minor (invention home). Re-scorable to
  2 violins, oboe+bassoon, etc.
- **Category:** `figuration` (both are figuration-beyond-arpeggio: the moving-line and counterpoint
  types the F-shelf was built for, sitting beside F2/F3/F4/F6). Card (b) also reads as `dialogue`
  (two independent voices) — cross-shelf, like D4.
- **Tempo:** moderately quick so 16ths *run* but stay melodic — q≈84–96 (a singable allegretto, not a
  blur). Faster reads as a blur/etude; slower kills the moto-perpetuo motion.
