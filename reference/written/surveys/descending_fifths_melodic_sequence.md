# The Descending-Fifths Melodic Sequence (survey, NOT distilled — library-build source)

The technique: **a single melodic line whose strong beats outline a root-motion-by-descending-fifth
progression** — the most directional engine in tonal music. Each chord's root sits a perfect 5th below
(or a P4 above) the previous one; the melody is built as a short cell that **transposes down by step**
each iteration, so the line *descends stepwise* in lock-step with the harmonic descent. This is BOTH the
source the `MEL2_DESC_FIFTHS` card is composed from AND a durable library reference. The detail is the
point — do not over-distill.

Companion craft: `melody_theory_foundations.md` §3 (circle of fifths, "root motion by descending 5th is
the strongest progression"; melodic vs. harmonic sequence) + §0 (the deep stepwise descent to ^1) +
`melody_progression_exemplars.md` (strong beats spell the chords). This survey supplies the *technique-
specific* mechanics those docs gesture at.

## 1. THE MECHANISM — WHY it pulls

`[research]` Root motion **down a perfect 5th** is the strongest, most "expected" progression in tonal
music (V→I is the cadence itself; ii→V, vi→ii… are the same move chained). Stringing them together
produces a sequence that has, in the textbook phrase, **"a feeling of inevitability"** — the ear hears
each chord *demand* the next, like a ball rolling downhill. It is the home idiom of the Baroque
(continuous, "spinning-out" Fortspinnung), and the structural skeleton of most jazz standards.

The WHY in one line: each chord is the **dominant-function approach to the next** (every down-5th is a
micro V→I), so the progression is a chain of resolutions that never quite lets you rest until the final
tonic. *Forward motion is built into the root motion itself*, which is exactly why a melody written over
it can be simple and still feel like it is *travelling* (the Zelda/Gold-Saucer "hero walking across a
changing map" lesson, foundations §3).

## 2. THE DIATONIC CHAIN (the full lap, and why you stop short)

`[research — Milne, *Fundamentals/Function/Form* ch.25; Puget Sound *Circle of Fifths*]`
The complete diatonic descending-fifth sequence beginning on the tonic visits **every** scale degree
once and returns:

- **Major:** `I – IV – vii° – iii – vi – ii – V – I` (degree roots `1 4 7 3 6 2 5 1`).
- **Minor:** `i – iv – VII – III – VI – ii° – V – i`.
- In **A minor** the roots spell **A–D–G–C–F–B–E–A**; in **E minor**, **E–A–D–G–C–F#–B–E**.

Note the chain is diatonic, so **one link is a diminished 5th, not perfect** (the ♭2→5 / ♭6→2 step
across the `vii°`/`ii°`), and the chord *qualities* shift through the key (minor, major, diminished) —
this variegated color is a feature, not an error. The leading-tone triad (`vii°` major / `ii°` minor)
is the pivot where the diminished link sits.

`[research]` Composers from the Baroque on **rarely run the whole lap** — a full circuit was "generally
considered monotonous by later composers." Real examples use **3–5 links** then break the pattern with a
cadence. The 8-bar card therefore runs the WHOLE lap precisely *because* a complete diatonic circuit
that begins and ends on the tonic is itself the textbook "complete progression" — but the line must keep
it from sounding mechanical (see §5).

## 3. THE MELODIC SIGNATURE — the line descends in lock-step

`[research — Milne ch.25, the Bach BWV593 example]` The defining *melodic* fact (not just harmonic):
as the roots descend by 5th, the **upper voice descends stepwise**, one step per harmonic unit. In the
cited Bach Organ Concerto in A minor passage the soprano

> begins on **F** (m.6), repeats on **E** (m.7) and **D** (m.8) before landing on **C** (m.9)

— i.e. a **short melodic cell transposed down by step** each iteration. That is the *melodic sequence*
half of the term: the pitch pattern is copied at a new level, a diatonic step lower, mirroring the
harmonic sequence beneath it. The listener locks onto the pattern and *predicts* the descent — the hook
is the recognition itself.

**Two-bar (or one-bar) model + copies.** The canonical realization: write a **model** over the first
chord (or first descending-5th pair) and then **sequence it down by step** for each subsequent link.
The model is usually a small idea — a falling third, a turn, a leap-and-step — kept short so the copies
are legible.

## 4. THE LINEAR INTERVALLIC PATTERNS (the idiomatic surface)

`[research — Milne ch.25, "linear intervallic patterns" / LIPs]` The outer-voice frame that voices the
sequence:

- **10–7 (the suspension chain):** "**tenths become suspended sevenths.**" The melody sits a 10th above
  the bass on each chord, then the bass moves down a 5th and the held melody note becomes a **7th**
  (a suspension) that **resolves down by step** to the next 10th — a chain of **7–6 / suspension**
  aches, each resolving down. *This is the single most idiomatic melodic flavor of the sequence:* a
  descending chain of prepared dissonances resolving downward. (foundations §5: suspension = the
  strongest "ache + release"; here it is chained.)
- **10–10 / falling-thirds outline:** if the melody simply tracks the bass in parallel 10ths, the upper
  line **arpeggiates downward in thirds** — another recognizable descending-fifths melodic outline.
- **5–6 and 6–10:** alternating root-position / first-inversion chords produce these; melodically they
  let the line **alternate leaps and steps** rather than pure stepwise descent — a way to keep the copies
  from being too plain.

So the melodist has a menu: pure stepwise descent (plain, hymnlike), a **7–6 suspension chain** (the
aching Baroque sigh), or a **falling-thirds arpeggiation** (brighter, more athletic). Mixing them across
the phrase keeps the lock-step sequence from droning.

## 5. KEEPING IT FROM DRONING — the craft problem this technique creates

`[synthesis from research + MELODY_CRAFT_DOCTRINE]` The danger is exactly the doctrine's **stencil
failure**: a sequence is by definition one cell stamped at descending levels, so a careless realization
is *literally* the thing the doctrine forbids — the same gesture eight times. The research's own verdict
("monotonous," "tedious") is the same warning. The counters:

1. **Vary the LIP across the chain** — start with the bright falling-thirds outline, tighten to the
   aching 7–6 suspension chain as energy gathers, so the *surface* develops even though the engine is
   constant.
2. **A single rationed apex**, leapt-to, that breaks the descent once (foundations §6) — the line cannot
   only descend or it is a slope, not an arch. Lift to the apex against the harmonic gravity, then let
   the sequence resume the descent toward the tonic.
3. **Rests / breath between copies** — silence is an accent (doctrine §7); a short breath before each new
   copy makes the sequence *phrase* instead of churn, and makes the next entry hit.
4. **Agogic landings** — hold the structural pitch of each link (the chord tone the copy lands on) so the
   sequence has weight, not just flow.
5. **Break the pattern at the cadence** — the last 1–2 links abandon the literal sequence for a real
   cadential gesture (V→I PAC), so the inevitability *arrives* rather than just stops. The diminished
   link (`vii°`/`ii°`) is a natural place to start bending the pattern.

## 6. REPERTOIRE ACROSS IDIOMS (the WHY, per item)

`[research]`
- **Baroque — the home idiom.** Bach, *Organ Concerto in A minor* BWV593/i mm.5–9 (the soprano F–E–D–C
  descent quoted in §3); Bach, *Brandenburg Concerto No.2* BWV1047/i; **Vivaldi** uses it constantly,
  "especially in minor," in the *Four Seasons* and the concerto ritornellos — the sequence IS the
  spinning-forth engine of the Vivaldian ritornello. **Pachelbel's Canon in D** is the popular emblem of
  descending root motion. WHY: the Baroque aesthetic of continuous, goal-directed motion ("inevitability")
  is precisely what chained dominant-resolutions deliver.
- **Jazz — the ii–V–I chain.** "**Autumn Leaves**" is "almost entirely made up of major and minor
  ii–V–I progressions," its bass "a pattern of ascending fourths / descending fifths" cycling through all
  seven scale degrees to the relative minor. Every ii–V–I is itself two descending fifths; chaining them
  is the descending-fifths sequence wearing a jazz hat. WHY: the same forward-pull, used as the harmonic
  highway a soloist's line travels over.
- **Classical/Romantic.** Used widely but **deliberately truncated** (3–4 links) after the Baroque,
  precisely because the full lap was felt as monotonous — the later aesthetic wanted the pull without the
  mechanism showing.
- **Film/game.** The "travelling" sensation (a stable melodic shape recolored by each new chord) is the
  adventure/journey cue par excellence — the harmony moves the listener through space under a singable
  line (foundations §3, the Zelda doctrine).

## 7. CONCRETE NOTE-LEVEL TEMPLATE (what the card builds on)

`[synthesis]` In **A minor**, a 7–6 suspension-chain realization of `i–iv–VII–III–VI–ii°–V–i` (roots
A–D–G–C–F–B–E–A), upper voice descending by step with each held note becoming a 7th over the next root:

| link | chord | bass root | held melody note → its function over the chord | resolves to |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | i (Am)   | A | E = 5th         | (start)      |
| 2 | iv (Dm)  | D | C → 7th of Dm (susp)   | B♭/B = 6th→… |
| 3 | VII (G)  | G | B → ... 3rd, or A held = 7th of... | down a step |
| 4 | III (C)  | C | descending copy a step lower | … |
| … | …        | … | each copy a diatonic step below the last | … |
| 7 | V (E)    | E | the dominant — raised leading-tone G# available | → ^1 |
| 8 | i (Am)   | A | ^2–^1 (B→A) PAC, re-struck tonic | close |

The takeaway the card must honor: **(a)** every strong beat lands a chord tone of that bar's descending-
5th root (so the bare strong-beat skeleton *names* the chain); **(b)** the melodic cell is recognizably
*the same cell transposed down a step* per link (the melodic sequence); **(c)** the surface develops
(LIP changes, one apex, breath, agogic holds) so it sings instead of droning; **(d)** the literal
sequence breaks for a real PAC at the end.
