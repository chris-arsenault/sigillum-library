# Outlining the Changes — survey (one melodic line that makes a chord progression audible with NO comping)

Research capture for the melody/line library. The technique: a SINGLE monophonic line — no
chords sounding under it — that nonetheless lets a listener hear the harmonic progression
unambiguously. This is the jazz/bebop craft of "playing the changes," "outlining the harmony,"
"spelling the chords." It is the positive twin of `harmonic_mislead.md`: that card studies how a
line *hides* its progression; this one studies the disciplined defaults a line uses to *expose*
its progression, note by note, beat by beat. The two share one mechanism (what lands on the
strong beat) pointed in opposite directions.

The brief asked for concrete, note-level rules — not vibes. Everything below is pitch-and-beat
specific. Sources fetched this run are cited inline. Richness is preserved deliberately; this is a
durable reference, not a checklist.

---

## 0. THE CENTRAL PRINCIPLE — harmonic resolution synchronized with metric resolution

The whole craft reduces to one alignment: **put chord tones on the strong beats, put non-chord
tones (approaches, passing, neighbor) on the weak beats/offbeats.** The ear's expectation of metric
arrival (beat 1, beat 3) and the ear's expectation of harmonic arrival (a stable chord tone) are
made to land at the same instant. The line then *sounds resolved when the harmony resolves* — and
because the only consonant anchors the ear can grab are the chord tones the line keeps planting on
the beat, the listener reconstructs the progression from those anchors alone.

Hal Galper calls this **Forward Motion**: phrases are heard as *motion toward future resolution
points*, and "all the downbeats should contain 'working' chord tones — 1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th."
[halgalper.com] Anton Schwartz: "target notes" (a.k.a. "landing notes") "carry much of the
phrase's meaning… target notes typically land on downbeats, while approach notes fill the space
before them off the beat." [antonjazz.com]

The four levers a single line pulls to make changes audible (in rough order of strength):

1. **Guide tones** — voice-lead the 3rds and 7ths (§1). Defines chord *quality* and motion.
2. **Chord-tone targeting on strong beats** — land 1/3/5/7 on beats 1 and 3 (§2).
3. **Arpeggiation + root placement** — spell the chord; place/imply the root (§3).
4. **Approaches & enclosures off the beat** — chromatic/diatonic notes that *target* chord tones,
   and deliberately sit on weak beats so they don't muddy the anchor (§4).

§5 assembles these over a ii–V–I and a turnaround. §6 is bebop-head practice. §7 is the takeaway.

## 1. GUIDE-TONE LINES — the 3rd and 7th carry the harmony

### 1.1 Why the 3rd and the 7th, specifically

A chord's root and 5th are *shared* across major / minor / dominant and so are nearly useless for
telling the listener which chord is sounding. The **3rd and 7th are the two notes that define chord
quality**: the 3rd says major-or-minor, the 7th says major/dominant/minor. Over a C root:
[learnjazzstandards.com]

- **Cmaj7** → 3rd = E (natural), 7th = B (natural)
- **Cm7** → 3rd = E♭ (lowered), 7th = B♭ (lowered)
- **C7 (dominant)** → 3rd = E (natural), 7th = B♭ (lowered)

The root (C) and 5th (G) are identical in all three; only the 3rd/7th pair distinguishes them. So a
line that reliably states the 3rd and 7th of each chord has *already told the listener the chord's
quality* before any root is played. They are called "guide" tones because they **guide the voice
leading** through the changes by the smallest possible motion. [learnjazzstandards.com,
guitarwiz.app]

### 1.2 The 7th-of-one → 3rd-of-the-next resolution (the engine of ii–V–I)

The defining event of jazz voice leading: **the 7th of each chord resolves DOWN by a half step to
become the 3rd of the next chord** (when the roots move down a 5th / up a 4th). Worked over a ii–V–I
in C major (Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7): [voicelidjazzguitar.com, interactivechordfinder.com,
learnjazzstandards.com]

| chord  | 3rd | 7th | what moves into the next chord |
|--------|-----|-----|--------------------------------|
| Dm7    | F   | C   | C (7th of Dm7) → B (3rd of G7), down a half step |
| G7     | B   | F   | F (7th of G7) → E (3rd of Cmaj7), down a half step |
| Cmaj7  | E   | B   | — (arrival) |

Notice the cross-stitch: in Dm7 the 7th is C and the 3rd is F. Moving to G7, **F stays put**
(common tone — it is now the 7th of G7) while **C drops a half step to B** (now the 3rd of G7).
Moving to Cmaj7, **B stays/holds** (now the 7th of Cmaj7) while **F drops to E** (now the 3rd of
Cmaj7). Each chord change, one guide tone is a common tone and the other slips a half step. That
relentless half-step descent of the 7th-into-the-3rd is the sound of "the changes resolving."

### 1.3 The two guide-tone lines (build either, then embellish)

From any ii–V–I you can extract **two** smooth guide-tone lines — one beginning on the 3rd of the
first chord, one on the 7th — each a near-stepwise melody. Over Dm7–G7–Cmaj7:
[voicelidjazzguitar.com]

- **Line A (start on the 3rd):** F (3 of Dm7) → B (3 of G7) → E (3 of Cmaj7)
- **Line B (start on the 7th):** C (7 of Dm7) → F (7 of G7→ also resolves) → B / E

The practical method Galper describes is exactly this: derive a **strong half-note melody from the
guide tones of the changes**, then embellish it with eighth-note runs and chromaticism. The
guide-tone line is the skeleton; everything decorative hangs off it. [halgalper.com]

> **Scholarly framing.** Smither's "Guide-Tone Space" (Music Theory Online 25.2) models exactly
> this: guide tones form a low-dimensional voice-leading space through which jazz changes move by
> minimal (half-step / common-tone) motion. The 3rd/7th pair is the load-bearing structure of
> functional jazz progressions. [mtosmt.org]

## 2. CHORD-TONE TARGETING ON STRONG BEATS

### 2.1 The rule

On the **downbeat of each chord** (and on beat 3 in a two-chords-per-bar tune), land a chord tone —
ideally a guide tone (3 or 7), else root or 5th. "On beat 1 or beat 3 of any measure you want one
of [1/3/5/7] under your fingers." [weissguitar.com, learnjazzstandards.com] Everything between
those anchors is connective tissue.

For an **unaccompanied** melodic line, the priority order shifts subtly: with no bass playing roots,
the 3rd and 5th on the downbeat are reported as the *clearest* for making a chord change audible in
a solo line — the 3rd because it states quality, the 5th/root because it states identity. Roots on
every downbeat is more an accompaniment (walking-bass) default than a soloing one. [bassmagazine.com
via Bass Magazine; talkbass.com] The point: a melodic line outlining changes alone should privilege
the **3rd** (quality) and clearly-placed **root** (identity) at the moment of change.

### 2.2 The bebop scale — engineering chord tones onto the downbeats

The bebop scales are seven-note scales with **one chromatic passing tone added** to make eight
notes. Eight eighth-notes fill exactly four beats, so when you run the scale in straight eighths,
**chord tones fall on every downbeat and the chromatic passing tone falls on an offbeat** — the
alignment of §0 happens automatically. [Wikipedia *Bebop scale*; pianowithjonny.com]

- **Bebop dominant** = Mixolydian + chromatic passing tone between ♭7 and root.
  C: **C D E F G A B♭ B C**. Descending in eighths from the root, the chord tones (root, ♭7, 5, 3)
  land on the beats; the added natural-7 (B) is the offbeat passing note. Used over dominant chords
  and over the V (and often the whole ii–V). [Wikipedia; learnjazzstandards.com]
- **Bebop major** = Ionian + ♯5 passing tone between 5 and 6.
  C: **C D E F G G♯ A B C**. Chord tones 1, 3, 5, 6 land on beats. Barry Harris's
  "major **6th-diminished** scale" is the same eight notes reframed as a C6 chord interleaved with
  a B°7. [Wikipedia; cochranemusic.com; jazz-guitar-licks.com]
- **Bebop minor / dorian** = passing tone added so 1, ♭3, 5, 6(or ♭7) land on beats. C melodic-minor
  bebop: C D E♭ F G G♯ A B C. [Wikipedia]

David Baker named these "bebop scales" after observing their constant use by Parker, Bud Powell,
Charlie Christian, Gillespie; Barry Harris built the 6th-diminished theory around the same idea —
a "scale of chords" where the 6th counts as a chord tone (like 1/3/5) and the added note is a color
tone (like 2/4). The Barry Harris rule of thumb: descending from a downbeat, chord tones stay on
the beats. [cochranemusic.com; insidepiano.com; freejazzlessons.com]

> **Caveat — don't make it mechanical.** Jens Larsen and others stress that "chromatic notes must
> be on the offbeat" is a *strong default, not a law*. Parker freely puts a chromatic note on a
> downbeat for color (e.g. a C♯ on D7, an F♯ on B♭maj7 in *Anthropology*) — the line still works
> because the surrounding melody resolves it. The default makes changes *clear*; breaking it
> knowingly adds tension. [jenslarsen.nl]

## 3. ARPEGGIATION AND THE ROOT

### 3.1 Arpeggiation spells the chord directly

The most literal way to outline a chord is to **arpeggiate it** — play 1-3-5-7 (or a fragment) in
sequence. A line that arpeggiates each chord in turn hands the listener the full quality. Bebop
heads lean on this heavily (§6). Parker's navigation devices are summarized as exactly this short
list: **enclosures, target notes, approach notes, chord arpeggios, and ii–V language.**
[jazzadvice.com]

Two refinements that keep arpeggios from sounding like exercises:

- **Start the arpeggio from the 3rd or 7th**, not always the root — you state the guide tone first
  (quality up front) and the root becomes an implication rather than a thud.
- **Use upper-structure / substitute arpeggios** to imply extensions: e.g. over Cmaj7 play an Em7 or
  G arpeggio (the 3-5-7-9 region) — you still spell C harmony but voice the colour tones. Galper's
  "superimposed chords." [halgalper.com]

### 3.2 Where the root goes (and when it's only implied)

In an unaccompanied single line there is no bass to state roots, so the line itself decides how much
root to give:

- **State the root at the moment of change** — root on the downbeat of a new chord is the
  unambiguous "we are now on X" signal; cheap and clear, used to nail a turnaround.
- **Imply the root** — once the 3rd and 7th are stated, the root is *harmonically redundant*; the
  ear supplies it. This is why guide-tone lines work without ever playing a root. A line can go
  whole phrases on 3rds/7ths/9ths and the root never sounds yet is unmistakably present.
- **Bass-style root-on-downbeat + chromatic walk-in**: when a tune moves in 4ths/5ths, place the
  root on each downbeat and lead into the *next* root with chromatic approach notes on the last
  beat(s) — this is the walking-bass outline, equally legible as a single line. [learnjazzstandards.com]

The strongest single-line outlining usually **mixes**: root or 3rd on the change to assert
identity, then guide tones / arpeggio fragments to carry quality, with the root dropped once it's
been established.

## 4. APPROACH NOTES & ENCLOSURES — targeting chord tones from off the beat

Approach notes are the connective tissue that gets the line from one strong-beat chord tone to the
next. The non-negotiable placement: **the target chord tone lands ON the beat; its approach
note(s) sit on the preceding offbeat(s).** That placement is *why* the approaches don't blur the
harmony — they're metrically subordinate, heard as decoration leaning into the anchor.
[jazz-guitar-licks.com; antonjazz.com]

### 4.1 The catalogue (target = a chord tone, e.g. C as root of Cmaj7)

- **Single diatonic approach** — one scale step adjacent to the target. Target C: approach from B
  (below) or D (above), using notes already in the key. Smoothest, most consonant.
- **Single chromatic approach** — the semitone neighbor. Target C: B (below) or D♭ (above), on the
  offbeat, resolving up/down to C on the beat. Approaching **from below** (the leading-tone effect)
  "flies under the radar"; chromatic-from-above is more conspicuous, so diatonic-from-above is often
  preferred. [antonjazz.com]
- **Double chromatic approach** — two chromatic notes walking into the target (e.g. B♭–B → C),
  especially idiomatic over dominant chords; pure bebop sound.
- **Enclosure (surround)** — approach the target from **both sides**, above and below, before
  landing. Target E (3rd of Cmaj7): play **F then D♯, then E** — F is the upper neighbor, D♯ the
  chromatic lower neighbor, E the on-beat resolution. A common mixed enclosure: *scale-tone above +
  chromatic-tone below* (e.g. target G: A then F♯ then G). Enclosures create maximum anticipation,
  then "home in on the target from both directions." [jazz-guitar-licks.com; fertilemindsjazzacademy;
  jazztricks.dk]

### 4.2 Why off-the-beat matters for outlining

Because the listener reconstructs the progression from the on-beat anchors, any note ON a strong
beat is read as harmony. Keep approaches/enclosures on weak beats and they read as *melodic*
embellishment around a clearly-stated chord tone — the harmony stays legible. Put them on the
downbeat and the ear may (re)interpret them as the chord (this is precisely the lever
`harmonic_mislead.md` pulls in the opposite direction). The bebop scale (§2.2) is just this rule
mechanized: the added chromatic note is *guaranteed* to fall offbeat. [jazz-guitar-licks.com;
Wikipedia *Bebop scale*]

## 5. ASSEMBLING IT — a ii–V–I and a turnaround, made unambiguous in one line

### 5.1 A ii–V–I in C (Dm7 | G7 | Cmaj7), one bar each, eighth notes

A textbook outlining line stacks all four levers. One legible realization (downbeats marked •):

```
Dm7 :  •F   A    •C   E      (arpeggio up from the 3rd: F=3, A=5, C=7, E=9 — quality stated)
G7  :  •F   E♭   •D   B      (F=7 held over, walk down ♭13→5→ to B=3 on beat 4 leading to...)
Cmaj7: •E   ...               (E = 3rd of C, the 7-of-G7→3-of-C resolution, landed on the downbeat)
```

What makes it unambiguous, mechanism by mechanism:
- **Guide tones**: Dm7 states C(7)+F(3); G7's downbeat F is the held 7th, its B is the 3rd; Cmaj7
  lands on E (3rd). The 7→3 half-step chain (C→B implied, F→E to the tonic) is audible. (§1)
- **Strong beats**: every downbeat is a chord tone (F, F, E). (§2)
- **Arpeggiation/root**: Dm7 bar arpeggiates the chord (quality explicit); roots are implied, not
  needed once 3/7 are stated. (§3)
- **Approach/enclosure**: the B before E (and any chromatic neighbor) sit on weak beats, targeting
  E. (§4)

### 5.2 The longer turnaround (I–vi–ii–V, e.g. Cmaj7 | A7 | Dm7 | G7)

A turnaround cycles fast (often two chords per bar), so the **guide-tone half-step chain does the
work** and the line privileges the 3rd of each chord on each change:

- Cmaj7: land **E** (3) → A7: land **C♯** (3, the chromatic lift that signals the secondary
  dominant — A7 not Am7) → Dm7: land **F** (3) → G7: land **B** (3) → back to Cmaj7's E.
- The 7→3 stitches: B(7 of C)→C♯(3 of A7); G(7 of A7)→F(3 of Dm7); C(7 of Dm7)→B(3 of G7); F(7 of
  G7)→E(3 of Cmaj7). Each change announced by a half-step slip into a quality-defining 3rd.

The **C♯** on the A7 downbeat is the load-bearing moment: it is outside C major, so the listener
*must* hear a new (dominant) chord — a single accidental on a strong beat makes the secondary
dominant audible. This is the constructive use of the same lever §4.2 warns about.

## 6. BEBOP-HEAD PRACTICE — the composed melody that IS the analysis

Bebop heads (the written tunes, not the solos) are the purest worked examples: a single line,
usually no comping under the statement at tempo, that spells the entire form. Ted Gioia called
Parker's *Confirmation* and *Donna Lee* "a primer in modern jazz phrase construction." [Wikipedia
*Confirmation*; jazzadvice.com]

- **Construction kit**: the heads are built from exactly the §1–§4 devices — "enclosures, target
  notes, approach notes, chord arpeggios, ii–V language." Phrases characteristically **begin on an
  upbeat and cross the bar line to resolve on a downbeat** (Galper's forward motion; the
  two-bar-phrase, upbeat-start norm noted in *Ornithology* analysis). [jazzadvice.com; halgalper.com;
  blog.uvm.edu]
- **Contrafacts prove the point**: *Ornithology* is a new bebop melody written over *How High the
  Moon*'s changes; *Donna Lee* over *Back Home in Indiana*; countless heads over rhythm changes
  (I-Got-Rhythm) and the blues. The composer keeps the harmony and replaces the tune with a line
  that outlines those same changes more explicitly — direct evidence that a single well-built line
  *encodes* the progression. [blog.uvm.edu; jazzadvice.com]
- **Confirmation specifically**: its hallmark is a chain of fast **ii–V walk-downs** (a cycle of
  descending dominants) plus a minor ii–V; the head threads a flowing line through that rapid
  harmonic motion using the 7→3 guide-tone connections to glue each ii–V to the next.
  [jazzadvice.com; Wikipedia]
- **Rhythm-changes / blues heads** show the same: the line arpeggiates and guide-tone-connects the
  I–vi–ii–V turnarounds so a listener hears the full 32-bar (or 12-bar) form from the melody alone.

The lesson for the library: a strong outlining line is *composed like a bebop head* — guide-tone
skeleton first, arpeggio fragments to state quality, chromatic enclosures/approaches off the beat to
connect, phrases launched on upbeats and resolved onto downbeat chord tones.

## 7. TAKEAWAY — the concrete process for writing a line that makes changes audible

1. **Lay the guide-tone skeleton first.** For each chord write its 3rd and 7th; connect them with
   the 7→3 half-step (one common tone, one half-step move per change). Pick the line (start on 3 or
   on 7) that flows most smoothly. This is the harmony, in two notes per chord. (§1)
2. **Anchor it to the meter.** Put a chord tone — preferably the guide tone, especially the **3rd**
   (quality) and the **root at the moment of change** (identity) — on the **downbeat of every
   chord** (and beat 3 if two chords/bar). (§2)
3. **State quality with arpeggio fragments**; start arpeggios from the 3rd/7th so the root can be
   implied rather than always played. Drop the root once it's established. (§3)
4. **Connect anchors with approaches/enclosures on the weak beats** — diatonic above, chromatic
   below, double-chromatic into dominants, surrounds (above+below) into important targets. Keep them
   OFF the strong beats so they read as decoration, not harmony. (§4)
5. **Use the bebop scale when running eighths** — its eight notes auto-place chord tones on
   downbeats and the chromatic note offbeat. (§2.2)
6. **Phrase like a bebop head**: launch phrases on an upbeat, cross the barline, resolve onto a
   downbeat chord tone. Announce secondary dominants / borrowed chords with the one accidental
   (e.g. C♯ on A7) placed deliberately on the strong beat. (§5–§6)

The line then carries its progression by itself: the strong beats spell the chords, the 7→3 chain
voices the motion, the offbeats decorate without lying — and a listener with no comping hears the
changes.
